66 / 76 / 89 — Single-engine

Vmca · Vsse · Vyse. Below 66 you can't hold heading on one engine. Below 76 you don't simulate an engine cut. At 89 you climb best on one engine. The whole story of asymmetric flight in three numbers.

107 / 121 / 129 / 138 — Configuration

Flaps: 10° — 138 KIAS, 25° — 121 KIAS, Full (40°) — 107 KIAS.
Gear: Extend and down — 129 KIAS; Retract — below 107 KIAS.
Memory: More flap, less speed. Gear down at 129, gear up by 107.

163 / 195 — Structure

Vno is where you stop pushing in turbulence. Vne is the wall. The 32-knot yellow band between them is for smooth air only.

85 — The abort floor

Not on the airspeed indicator, but lives in your head. Engine fails on takeoff below 85, you close throttles and land — period. Above 85, runway and conditions become a decision.

Module 01 · Powerplant Group

Powerplant

Engine, turbocharger, induction, fuel, oil, propellers, governors — and the single-engine handling that depends on all of them working together.

Two Continental TSIO-360 engines drive this airplane. They are counter-rotating — left rotates clockwise, right counter-clockwise, viewed from the cockpit — which eliminates the critical-engine concept. Each engine is a self-contained system with its own fuel path, oil sump, alternator, instrument-air pump, and propeller governor. But the components within each engine are tightly interlocked: fuel needs the engine to spin its pump, the pump needs combustion, combustion needs air through the turbo and metered fuel from injection, oil pressure controls propeller pitch, and the propeller is what turns all of it back into thrust. Pull any one thread and the whole system shifts. That's why we study them as a group.

Diagram 1.1 How it all connects

Powerplant flow · air, fuel, oil, exhaust

Components Six interlocking subsystems

Engine
Continental TSIO-360E/EB (left, clockwise rotation) and LTSIO-360E/EB (right, counter-clockwise). Six-cylinder, horizontally opposed, air-cooled, fuel-injected, turbocharged.
  • 200 hp at sea level / 215 hp at 12,000 ft
  • 2575 RPM max · 7.5:1 compression · 360 in³ displacement
  • Counter-rotation eliminates critical engine
  • Dynafocal mounts · removable cowl panels for inspection
Turbocharger
Ray-Jay exhaust-driven. Compressor pressurizes induction air. No automatic boost controller — the throttle is your only boost limit at low altitude.
  • 40" Hg manual ceiling · 42" Hg pressure relief valve
  • Fixed bypass orifice preset for 40" at 12,000 ft DA
  • Overboost annunciator illuminates ~39.8" Hg
  • Smooth throttle movement only — let turbo speed stabilize
Induction & Alternate Air
Primary path is filtered cowl air. Alternate air bypasses the filter and draws unfiltered, heated air from inside the cowling. A suck-in door auto-opens if the primary blocks.
  • Alt air ON: induction icing, blocked filter, restart attempts
  • Alt air OFF: takeoff (always), ground in dust
  • Alt air is unfiltered — heated to melt ice
  • Suck-in door = automatic fail-safe (no pilot action)
Fuel
Two interconnected tanks per side act as one cell. Engine-driven pump primary; electric aux pump secondary (LO for vapor suppression, HI for engine-pump backup or priming, guarded). Continuous-flow injection with vapor return to the on-side tank.
  • 93 / 123 gal useable (standard / optional) · 5 gal unusable total
  • 100 / 100LL aviation grade
  • Crossfeed in level flight only · opposite selector OFF
  • 8 drains: 2 gascolator · 4 wing · 2 crossfeed (under fuselage, inboard of right flap)
Oil
Wet sump, pressure-fed. Engine-mounted filter, oil cooler with low-temperature bypass. Oil pressure also drives the propeller governor — so a single oil-system loss affects two systems.
  • 8 qt per engine · capacity range 6–8 qt operational
  • SAE 30 below 40°F OAT · SAE 50 above (multi-vis OK)
  • Winterization plate — remove above 50°F OAT
  • Min 10 PSI · max 100 PSI · max temp 240°F
Propellers & Governors
Both prop types: oil pressure drives toward HIGH RPM (fine pitch). Hartzell two-blade uses nitrogen + counterweights toward low RPM/feather. McCauley three-blade uses a compression spring + counterweights. Loss of oil pressure tends to feather the prop in this airplane.
  • Feathering takes ~6 seconds
  • Centrifugal lock prevents feather below 800 RPM
  • Synchrophaser MANUAL/OFF for taxi, takeoff, landing, single-engine
  • Avoid 2000–2200 RPM > 32" continuous · avoid 1700–2100 ground in cross/tail wind > 10 kt

Emergencies · powerplant When something goes wrong

Every engine emergency follows the same logic: aviate, identify, decide, secure. Maintain control first. Identify which engine. Decide whether to restore or secure. Then complete the securing sequence. The memory drills below give you the items and the decision points.

Engine Securing · Feathering Memory
After failure is identified, restoration attempts have failed, RPM still above 800.

Restore first

  1. Mixture — as required
  2. Fuel selector — CROSSFEED
  3. Magnetos — left or right only (mag check)
  4. Alternate air — ON
  5. Aux fuel pump — unlatch, HI · if no power restored, OFF

If no restart · feather (above 800 RPM, ≥ 76 KIAS)

  1. Mixture · Prop · Throttle — all forward (40" max)
  2. Flaps · Gear — retract
  3. Identify inop engine — yaw direction = nose toward dead
  4. Throttle inop — retard (verify loss of power)
  5. Mixture inop — idle cut-off
  6. Prop inop — FEATHER
  7. Trim — 5° bank toward operative engine
  8. Aux pumps — OFF (except eng-pump fail on operating side)
  9. Magnetos inop — OFF
  10. Cowl flap inop — close · operative as required
  11. Alternator inop — OFF
  12. Electrical load — reduce
  13. Fuel selector inop — OFF · operative on X-FEED if needed
After feather, ALT, GYRO, and OIL annunciator lights stay on for the dead engine — that's normal. The pumps and alternator on that side are no longer being driven.
Engine Failure on Takeoff Below 85 KIAS
Power loss during ground roll or just after lift-off, gear still down.
  1. Throttles — CLOSE both immediately
  2. Land if airborne
  3. Stop straight ahead
  4. If runway inadequate — max braking · master OFF · fuel selectors OFF
Below 85 KIAS the airplane cannot achieve a positive single-engine climb gradient with gear down, possible flaps, and a windmilling prop. Cleanup takes time you don't have.
Engine Failure on Takeoff 85 KIAS or above
Power loss during takeoff with 85+ KIAS achieved, gear still down.

Adequate runway: Close both throttles, land, stop straight ahead.

Inadequate runway: Pilot decision based on load, density altitude, obstructions, weather, and competence.

If continuing: Maintain heading and airspeed · feather inop engine · retract gear when climb is established.

During short-field takeoff with 25° flaps, the airplane is momentarily below Vmc. If the engine fails below Vmc it is mandatory to retard the operating throttle and lower the nose immediately to maintain control.
Engine Failure in Flight Above 66 KIAS
Power loss in cruise, climb, or descent with airspeed above Vmc.
  1. Rudder — toward operating engine
  2. Identify — inop engine ("dead foot, dead engine")
  3. Operative engine — adjust as required
  4. Restart attempt (≥ 76 KIAS): fuel flow → aux HI then OFF if no help · fuel quantity · selector X-FEED · alt air ON · mixture · oil P&T · mag switches
  5. If no restart — secure per Engine Securing drill
  6. Operative engine — power, mixture for power, fuel, aux as required, cowl flap as required
  7. Trim — 5° bank toward operative
  8. Electrical load — reduce
  9. Land as soon as practical at nearest suitable airport
Engine Failure in Flight Below 66 KIAS
Power loss with airspeed below Vmc — directional control is at risk.
  1. Rudder — toward operative engine
  2. Both throttles — retard to stop yaw
  3. Pitch — lower nose to accelerate above 66 KIAS
  4. Operative engine — increase power as airspeed exceeds 66
  5. Restart if altitude permits, ≥ 76 KIAS
  6. If no restart — feather · complete securing · trim 5° bank toward operative
You cannot restore directional control with full asymmetric power below Vmc. Reducing power on the operating engine eliminates the yaw moment so you can fly the airplane while you accelerate.
Engine Failure in Climb Memory

Below 66 KIAS: Rudder toward operating · reduce throttles for control · lower nose to accelerate to 89 KIAS · increase operating-engine power above 66 · feather inop.

Above 66 KIAS: Maintain directional control · adjust to 89 KIAS · feather inop.

Engine Fire in Flight Memory · affected engine
  1. Fuel selector — OFF
  2. Throttle — close
  3. Propeller — FEATHER
  4. Mixture — idle cut-off
  5. Heater — OFF
  6. Defroster — OFF
  7. Land if terrain permits if fire continues
Each step removes one element — fuel, air, ignition, heat. The heater is a combustion heater that runs on the same fuel system; both heater and defroster come off in any fire.
Engine Fire on Ground Start sequence

Engine not started yet: Mixture idle cut-off · throttle open · crank with starter (try to draw fire into engine).

Engine running: Continue operating to draw fire in.

If fire continues: Extinguish externally · fuel selectors OFF · mixture idle cut-off.

Engine-Driven Fuel Pump Failure Memory
Falling fuel flow, power loss, but engine still running.
  1. Throttle — retard
  2. Aux fuel pump — unlatch, HI
  3. Throttle — reset at 75% power or below
Aux pump on HI can deliver fuel for up to ~75% power. Lean as required at altitudes above 15,000 ft or below 2300 RPM. If no fuel-flow indication appears on HI, suspect a fuel leak or fuel exhaustion — turn aux OFF.
Air Start · Unfeathering Memory
Restoring a previously feathered engine in flight.
  1. Fuel selector inop engine — ON
  2. Aux fuel pump inop engine — OFF
  3. Throttle — open ¼ inch
  4. Prop control — forward to cruise RPM
  5. Mixture — RICH
  6. Magnetos — ON
  7. Starter — engage until prop windmills
  8. Throttle — reduce power until engine warm · prime if needed
  9. Alternator — ON
Engine Failure in Icing Memory
  1. Alternate air — ON · attempt restart
  2. If no start — feather the inop propeller
  3. Airspeed — at or above 89 KIAS · descend if needed
  4. Electrical load — reduce
  5. Avoid further icing · land as soon as practical
  6. Final approach — at least 89 KIAS · 25° flaps (not full)
Don't extend gear or flaps until certain of making the field — you may need every knot of energy in the descent.
Propeller Overspeed School-taught · not in POH §3
  1. Throttle — reduce (lowers slipstream energy)
  2. Prop control — full aft (commands low RPM)
  3. Airspeed — reduce
  4. If overspeed continues — treat as engine securing situation · feather if RPM control is lost
Both prop types resist overspeed mechanically — Hartzell via nitrogen + counterweights, McCauley via spring + counterweights. A true overspeed implies governor failure flooding oil into the hub or mechanical failure.

Concept Why single-engine is hard

The Seneca II is a docile light twin, but every twin is uncompromising about one thing: asymmetric thrust. With one engine producing full power and the other producing drag (windmilling prop, no thrust), the airplane wants to yaw toward the dead engine. Three things have to be true to maintain controlled flight:

  1. Enough airspeed for the rudder to bite (above Vmc).
  2. Enough bank toward the operating engine (5°) to use horizontal lift to offset the yaw.
  3. The dead-engine prop feathered — windmilling drag is the largest drag source on the airplane.

Lose any one of those and you're working below the airplane's design margin. Lose all three and you're in a Vmc roll or spin — recovery may not be possible at low altitude.

The Seneca II altitude paradox. In a normally-aspirated twin, Vmc decreases with altitude because engine power falls off. In the turbocharged Seneca II, the engine maintains full rated power up to ~12,000 ft — so the asymmetric thrust doesn't go away with altitude, and Vmc can be higher up high than down low. The most dangerous altitude is where Vmc and stall speed converge — directional control loss happens at the same instant as the stall, and a spin can result. This is why §4.49 has you reduce power on the demo engine rather than use full takeoff power, and why the demo is done above 3500 ft AGL.

For the same reason, the abbreviated emergency checklist on this airplane keeps you above 76 KIAS (Vsse) for any deliberate engine cut, and 85 KIAS as the abort floor on takeoff. These aren't arbitrary numbers — they're the buffer between you and the spin.

Module 02 · Landing Gear & Hydraulics

Gear & Hydraulics

Hydraulic power pack · electric reversible pump · squat switch · indicator system · emergency free-fall extension. No mechanical backup — gravity is your backup.

The Seneca II uses a self-contained hydraulic power pack in the nose: pump, motor, reservoir, valves, all in one assembly. The pump is reversible — it can pressurize either the up-side or the down-side of the gear cylinders depending on selector position, eliminating the need for two pumps. There is no mechanical hand-pump. If the electrics fail, the gear extends by gravity, airflow, and downlock springs when you pull the emergency extension knob and dump system pressure.

Diagram 2.1 Gear control logic

Gear control · normal hydraulic and emergency free-fall paths

Components What's in the system

Hydraulic Power Pack
Self-contained unit in the nose — accessible through the forward baggage compartment. Pump, motor, reservoir, control valves, all integrated. Reversible: same pump pressurizes up-side or down-side depending on selector.
Gear Selector
Wheel-shaped knob, panel left of throttle quadrant. Pull-out before moving UP/DOWN. Two positions only.
Squat Switch
On the left main gear oleo strut. Open on the ground (oleo compressed) · breaks pump electrical circuit · prevents inadvertent retraction. Closes inflight when oleo extends.
Indicator Lights
Three green = down and locked. One red = transit or unsafe. Auto-dim with nav lights — common daytime trap when greens look "off" but are just dimmed.
Gear Horn
90 cycles/minute beep. Triggers when (a) MP < ~14" Hg on either engine with gear not down/locked, or (b) selector UP on the ground.
Emergency Extension Knob
Below the selector, behind a hinged clip. Pulling it dumps hydraulic pressure holding the gear up. Gravity, airflow, and downlock springs complete the extension. No way to retract by hand after pulling.
Convex Mirror
On the left engine nacelle. Lets you visually confirm the nose gear at night or when in doubt about an indication.
Brake System
Independent hydraulic reservoir (separate from the gear power pack), located behind a panel at the top rear of the nose-baggage compartment. Toe brakes on both pilot and copilot pedals. Hand-operated lever doubles as parking brake.

Emergencies · gear When the gear misbehaves

Manual Gear Extension Memory · 85 KIAS max
Normal extension fails — red light stays on, no greens after selector DOWN, or hydraulic system inoperative.
  1. Circuit breakers — check
  2. Master switch — ON
  3. Alternators — check
  4. Nav lights — OFF (daytime · so you can see green lights)
  5. Clip — reposition downward · clear of the knob
  6. Airspeed — reduce · ≤ 85 KIAS
  7. Gear selector — GEAR DOWN LOCKED position
  8. Pull the emergency gear extension knob
  9. Confirm 3 greens
  10. Leave knob out until airplane is on jacks
Pulling the knob releases the hydraulic pressure that holds the gear up. Gravity drops the mains and nose, airflow assists the nose gear, and downlock springs engage the locks. The selector goes to DOWN LOCKED so the pump won't try to retract.
Gear Light Won't Illuminate Troubleshoot
Selector DOWN, but only two greens (or fewer) appear.
  1. Nav lights — OFF (greens auto-dim with nav lights — daytime trap)
  2. Bulb swap — pull the dim/dark light, swap with a known-good one (they're interchangeable)
  3. If still dark — gear may not actually be locked · recycle gear
  4. If still dark after recycle — manual extension · prepare for possible gear-up landing
Three causes for one missing green: actually not locked, bulb burned out, or indicator-system fault. The bulb swap is the cheapest test. Nav-light dimming is the most common false alarm.
Gear Unsafe Light Memory
Red light stays on after selector movement · pump still cycling beyond 7 seconds.
  1. Recycle the gear if continued red light
  2. If still red after recycle — proceed to manual extension
  3. Anticipate horn at low throttle on approach if gear is not down/locked
Gear-Up Emergency Landing Memory
Manual extension fails or one or more gears confirmed not locked.
  1. Approach — with power · normal speed · flaps UP (reduce flap and wing damage)
  2. Just before touchdown — close throttles
  3. Master & ignition — OFF
  4. Fuel selectors — OFF
  5. Touch down at minimum airspeed

Module 03 · Electrical System

Electrical

Twin 65-amp alternators · single 35 Ah battery · 14-volt bus · solid-state regulators · overvoltage protection · external power option. Two paths to power, one path to ground.

The Seneca II runs a 14-volt DC system designed for full night IFR operation. Each engine drives its own alternator, but only one battery exists. That redundancy choice — two alternators, one battery — is fine in normal operation and during single-alt failure, but it sets up the dual-alt failure as a battery-only emergency where the duration depends on what loads are running. Memorize the duration numbers; an examiner will probe them.

Diagram 3.1 Power distribution

Electrical · two alternator chains feeding a single regulated bus

Components Power sources and protection

Battery
35 ampere-hour, 12 volt, in the nose section, accessible through the forward baggage compartment. Charged externally when removed. Sole power source for engine start (or via PEP).
Alternators · 2 ×
Each rated 65 A, engine-driven. ~2000 RPM needed for full output — zero output at idle is normal. Independent of one another up to the bus.
Voltage Regulators · 2 ×
Solid-state · regulate output to 14 V. Forward of the cabin/nose bulkhead.
Overvoltage Relays · 2 ×
Trip when bus voltage exceeds 14 V. Trips that side's alternator off line and illuminates the ALT annunciator. Reset by cycling the alternator switch OFF then ON.
Master Switch
Left switch panel. Energizes master solenoid · connects battery to bus. Independent ALT switches sit alongside.
Radio Master
Older airplanes: OFF / NORMAL / AUXILIARY (auxiliary = backup avionics power circuit). Later airplanes: NORMAL / OFF + a separate Emergency Bus Switch (left of CB panel, behind shin guard) that bypasses the radio master to power avionics directly.
Ammeters · 2 ×
Show alternator output (not battery current). At run-up should read approximately equal between sides. Full charge at run-up = undercharged battery · investigate before flight.
Annunciator Panel
Upper-left of radios. ALT (each), MP overboost, OIL pressure, GYRO pressure. Push-to-test. Aux fuel pump amber lights are tested separately via the primer switches — not by the panel test.
Hot Bus Loads
Cabin courtesy lights (over each cabin door, optional) and nose-baggage compartment light (auto-on when door opens). Both are independent of the master switch — leave them on overnight and you depleted the battery.
External Power · PEP
Optional. Receptacle on left side of nose. Master OFF for normal external start. After start: throttle low · disconnect plug · master ON · check ammeter · oil pressure.

Emergencies · electrical When the bus misbehaves

Single Alternator Failure Memory
One ALT light illuminates · one ammeter shows zero or abnormal output.
  1. Observe ammeters — identify the inop alternator
  2. If one ammeter zero — cycle that alt switch OFF then ON (resets OV relay)
  3. If still inop — check & reset CB once
  4. If still inop — reduce loads if needed · continue · land at suitable airport
  5. If both ammeters show high (much above known load) for > 5 min — suspect a fault other than the alternators
Both Alternators Off Line · "Both ALT lights" Memory
Both ammeters reading zero (or near zero), both ALT lights on.
  1. Reduce all loads to minimum
  2. Both alt switches — OFF
  3. Cycle each switch ON momentarily one at a time · observe ammeter
  4. Leave ON the alt with least but not zero output
  5. Other alt — OFF
  6. Re-establish loads up to 60 A
  7. If neither restores — proceed to Dual Alternator Failure
"Least but not zero" is the better-functioning alternator. Both showing identical zero suggests systemic — battery only.
Dual Alternator Failure · Battery Only Memory
No alternator output recoverable. All electrical now coming from the battery alone.
  1. Reduce all unnecessary load immediately
  2. Estimate available time — ~115 min day VFR · ~35 min night IFR
  3. Land as soon as practical at the nearest suitable airport
  4. If battery depletes en route — gear may need free-fall extension · greens may not illuminate
Battery time is finite. Plan to land before depletion, not after. Compass errors compound the navigation problem in IMC — get pointed at a divert before things degrade.
Alternator Failure in Icing Memory
ALT light(s) illuminate while operating in icing conditions with deice loads on.
  1. Reset overvoltage relay — cycle alt switch OFF/ON
  2. Check / reset CBs
  3. If alt cannot be restored: avionics — leave only one NAV/COM and the transponder ON
  4. Electric windshield — OFF · maintain load < 65 A
  5. If icing continues — terminate flight ASAP
  6. Prior to landing — electric windshield ON if necessary for visibility
  7. If battery depletes — gear may need free-fall
Heated windshield is the largest single load on the airplane. Drop it first; pick it back up only when you need to see for landing.

Module 04 · Flight Instruments & Air Data

Flight Instruments

Pitot-static · gyro pressure system · alternate static · annunciator panel. Two pneumatic sources you cannot confuse on the oral: pitot/static is air the airplane flies through, gyro pressure is air pumped through the gyros.

Two completely separate pneumatic systems share the cockpit. The pitot-static system feeds the airspeed indicator, altimeter, and VSI from the airplane's relative motion through the air. The gyro pressure system feeds the directional gyro and attitude indicator from engine-driven pumps that push air through the instrument cases. Critically — and this trips up new Seneca pilots — these are positive pressure gyros, not vacuum. If you say "vacuum system" on the oral, you'll get corrected.

Module 05 · Environmental & Ice Protection

Environmental & Ice

Janitrol combustion heater · ventilation · defrost · pneumatic boots · electrothermal prop deice · heated windshield, pitot, and lift detectors. The seven items that make this airplane legal in known icing.

The cabin heat system burns aircraft fuel — about 0.5 GPH from the left tank — through a Janitrol combustion heater in the aft fuselage. The same airplane also has the option of a complete ice protection system that combines pneumatic boots (driven by the gyro pumps' suction side), electrothermal propeller boots, an electric windshield panel, and heated probes. All seven items must be installed and serviceable for the airplane to be legally dispatched into known icing.

5.1 · Heating, Ventilation, Defrost Janitrol combustion heater

Janitrol Combustion Heater
Aft fuselage. Burns aircraft fuel (~0.5 GPH) drawn from the left tank via dedicated electric pump. Cycles burner to maintain set cabin temperature. Three-position control: FAN / OFF / HEATER.
Air Intake Lever
On console between front seats. Two safety switches at the intake valve prevent fan or heater from running with intake closed.
Inflight Blower Cutoff
A micro-switch tied to gear retraction turns the ventilation blower OFF in flight — cabin air comes from ram pressure once airborne.
Defroster Fan
Boosts windshield airflow on demand. Separate switch.
Overheat Switch + Light
Forward outboard end of heater vent jacket. Automatic cutoff: kills fuel, air, ignition. OVERHEAT light on console. Reset button on heater shroud accessible only through aft cabin closeout panel.
Combustion heater above 25,000 ft — not approved. The airplane itself is limited to 25,000 ft, so this is consistent. The heater also has a hard overheat-cutoff that you cannot reset inflight without aft-cabin access.

How the boots work · pressure or suction?

The pneumatic boots run on the suction side of the same engine-driven pumps that pressurize the gyro instruments. In their default state, suction holds the boots flat against the leading edges. When you press the SURFACE DE-ICE momentary switch, a 6-second cycle pressurizes the boots, breaking ice off the leading edge. After 6 seconds, suction returns and the boots streamline. The same pumps serve gyros (pressure) and boots (suction) — they are not redundant systems.

How the prop deice cycles

Electrothermal pads bonded to the blade leading edges, energized through brushes and slip rings.

  • Hartzell two-blade: heats outboard halves first, then inboard halves, alternating R then L. Sequence: R-out → R-in → L-out → L-in.
  • McCauley three-blade: heats entire R prop pads (90 sec), then entire L (90 sec).

Heat reduces blade-ice adhesion · centrifugal force throws ice clear. The prop deicer ammeter needle should read in the shaded portion of the gauge during operation.

Don't cycle boots below ¼ inch ice accumulation. Thin ice can be inflated through the boot cycle without breaking, then re-freeze on top — known as ice bridging. Don't wait past ½ inch either — boot inflation may not break the bond. The sweet spot is ¼ to ½ inch.

Module 06 · Flight Controls

Flight Controls

All-moving stabilator · Frise ailerons · mechanical flaps · cable-actuated. Conventional, predictable, no autopilot in the base POH.

The Seneca II uses dual yokes connected by cables and bellcranks to the primary control surfaces. The empennage uses an all-moving stabilator (slab type) with an anti-servo / trim tab on the trailing edge — the tab moves in the same direction as the stabilator, larger travel, to add longitudinal stability and prevent over-sensitivity. Ailerons are Frise type — leading edge extends into the airstream when deflected upward, adding drag and reducing adverse yaw.

Procedures

General Emergencies

Emergencies that don't belong to one specific system: emergency descent, spin recovery, door open in flight, engine failure with cabin/cargo doors removed.

Emergency Descent Memory
Rapid descent required — fire, decompression, oxygen failure at altitude.
  1. Throttles — closed
  2. Propellers — full FORWARD
  3. Mixture — as required for smooth operation
  4. Landing gear — extend
  5. Airspeed — 129 KIAS
Gear extension at 129 (Vle/Vlo) adds drag without overstressing. Props full forward provides additional drag. Time of useful consciousness at 25,000 ft is ~3 minutes — descend to ≤ 12,500 ft on oxygen-system failure.
Spin Recovery Memory · spins prohibited
Inadvertent spin entry · airplane is not certified for intentional spins.
  1. Throttles — idle
  2. Rudder — full opposite spin direction
  3. Control wheel — release back pressure · if nose doesn't drop, push full forward
  4. Ailerons — neutral
  5. Rudder — neutral when rotation stops
  6. Recover from dive with smooth back pressure · do not exceed 3.8 g
Spins are placarded prohibited and demonstrated SE stall recovery requires 500 ft loss and 15° pitch — assume spin entry will lose more.
Door Open on Takeoff or in Flight Memory
Door pops open in flight — usually noisy and surprising, rarely actually dangerous.
  1. Fly the airplane normally · this is not an immediate emergency
  2. Best plan: return to land and close on the ground
  3. If desired to close inflight: airspeed 85–94 KIAS · open storm window · pull door closed · latch upper
  4. If unclosable: airspeed 85–107 KIAS to avoid buffet · flight can continue safely
An open door is loud but the airplane flies normally. Don't get distracted from flying. Most pilots fixate and forget gear, flaps, or speed — this is the trap, not the door itself.
Engine Failure · Doors Removed Vmca = 67 KIAS
Engine failure during operations with rear cabin or cargo doors removed (skydiving, photo, etc.).
  1. Vmca becomes 67 KIAS in this configuration
  2. Below 67 — reduce power on operating engine, apply rudder for control
  3. Otherwise treat as normal engine failure
Removed doors disturb airflow over the empennage, slightly degrading rudder authority. The 1-knot Vmca penalty reflects that.

MP < ~14" Hg on either engine with gear not locked · OR selector UP on the ground
90 cycles per minute beep · different from continuous-tone stall warning.
Electrical system
Hot bus loads
Cabin courtesy lights (front & rear cabin door) · nose-baggage compartment light
Independent of master switch. Both will deplete the battery if left on.
Maximum continuous alternator output
65 A each (two alternators)
Single-alt operation: keep loads < 65 A · 60 A after isolation procedure. ~2000 RPM needed for full output — zero at idle is normal.
Both overvoltage / ALT lights illuminate
Reduce loads · both alts OFF · cycle each ON one at a time · leave on the alt with least but not zero output · loads up to 60 A · if neither restores, dual-alt failure procedure
Battery time alone: ~115 min day VFR · ~35 min night IFR. Compass error may exceed 10°.

Procedures
When to retract gear after takeoff
When a gear-down landing on remaining runway is no longer possible
Engine failure right after lift-off, gear is your friend if there's runway left. Once that's gone, gear is drag.
Normal flap setting for takeoff
0° (flaps up)
25° is short-field only · puts you momentarily below Vmc.
When to transition to climb power
When adequate terrain clearance is obtained
Cruise climb is 31.5" / 2450 RPM · 102 KIAS · mixture full rich.
Approach speeds & flaps
Downwind 98 KIAS · 0–10° · Base 97 / 25° · Final 87 / full
⚠ schoolSchool-taught 83 KIAS final and 79 KIAS lightly-loaded final are not in this POH (POH says 87). Defend the source.
Go-around procedure
Full takeoff power both engines (40 max) · positive climb · flaps retract · gear UP · cowl flaps adjust
Avoid single-engine go-around if possible — power application sharply increases control difficulty.
Emergencies
When use alternate air
Suspected induction icing · filter blockage · engine roughness in IMC/visible moisture · engine failure in icing · run-up cycle check
Don't use on takeoff (always primary filtered) · don't use on ground in dust (alt air is unfiltered).
Restart procedure prior to feathering
Mixture · selector X-FEED · mags L or R · alt air ON · aux pump HI then OFF if no help · maintain ≥ 76 KIAS
Each step tests one possible cause: F/A ratio, fuel supply, ignition, induction, engine-pump. None work → secure and feather.
Why abort below Vyse even if airborne
Below 85 KIAS the airplane cannot achieve a positive single-engine climb gradient with gear down, possible flaps, and a windmilling prop
By the time you've cleaned up the airplane and feathered, you'd be below Vyse without the altitude to recover. POH mandates throttles closed below 85; above 85 it's pilot decision.
Engine fire in flight
Fuel selector OFF · throttle close · prop FEATHER · mixture idle cut-off · heater OFF · defroster OFF · land if terrain permits if fire continues
Each step removes one element. Heater is a combustion heater on the same fuel.
Electrical failure in flight
Single alt: cycle switch · reset CB once · reduce loads · continue. Both: cycle switches one at a time, leave on the better one. Both inop: battery only · ~115 min day VFR / ~35 min night IFR · land ASAP.
In icing: drop the heated windshield first (largest single load). Compass error may exceed 10° on battery alone.
Propeller overspeed
Throttle reduce · prop full aft · airspeed reduce · feather if RPM control lost
⚠ schoolNot a discrete §3 procedure in this POH. School-taught. Both prop types resist mechanical overspeed (Hartzell nitrogen / McCauley spring).
Vmc
Minimum altitude for Vmc demo
3500 ft AGL
Per §4.49. Demo approaches uncontrolled flight; this altitude gives spin/stall recovery margin.
Vmc recovery procedure
Reduce power on the operating engine · lower the nose · accelerate to Vsse 76 KIAS
Asymmetric thrust IS the cause — reduce thrust to restore controllability. Lowering the nose recovers airspeed. Re-apply power above Vsse.
Factors affecting Vmc
Power · altitude · bank angle · weight · CG · configuration · prop state (windmill vs feather) · OAT/density · operating-engine power available
Seneca quirk: turbocharged engine maintains rated power up to 12,000 ft, so Vmc doesn't drop with altitude like normally-aspirated twins. The most dangerous altitude is where Vmc and stall speed converge — Vmc demo done with reduced power above 3500 AGL for that reason.
