Theory of flight ✓ Strict-sourced v2

Lift, drag, the four forces, stability, load factor. The physics that govern every other section in this document.

An airplane stays in the air because the wing produces lift greater than the airplane's weight, and the engine produces thrust greater than the airframe's drag. When either of those becomes untrue, the airplane descends — controlled or not. Every other concept in this document — performance, stalls, V-speeds, fuel planning, weather effects on aircraft — connects back to these basics. FTGU §1 · §2

Concept 1The four forces

In steady level flight, four forces act on the airplane and balance:

  • Lift — perpendicular to the relative airflow, produced by the wing
  • Weight — vertically downward, equal to the aircraft's mass × g
  • Thrust — along the flight path, produced by the propeller (or jet)
  • Drag — opposite to the flight path, the resistance to forward motion

FTGU §1.1

In steady flight: lift = weight, thrust = drag. Change any one and the airplane accelerates in that direction until equilibrium is restored.

Concept 2How a wing makes lift

Two complementary explanations, both physically correct: FTGU §1.x

  • Newton's third law. The wing deflects air downward; the air pushes the wing upward. The downward momentum imparted to the air equals the upward force on the wing.
  • Bernoulli's principle. Air flowing faster over the curved upper surface of the wing has lower static pressure than slower air below. The pressure difference produces an upward force.

Both explanations describe the same phenomenon. Pilots commonly use whichever is more convenient for the question being asked.

Lift formula: L = ½ × ρ × V² × S × CL

  • ρ (rho) — air density
  • V — airspeed (true)
  • S — wing surface area
  • CL — lift coefficient (depends on angle of attack and airfoil)

FTGU §1.x (lift formula)

Pilots can change three of these in flight: airspeed (throttle and pitch), angle of attack (elevator), and density indirectly via altitude. Wing area is fixed (except where flaps change it slightly).

Concept 3Drag — two types and the curve

Drag has two components: FTGU §1.x

  • Parasite drag. Resistance from the airframe moving through the air — friction drag, form drag, interference drag. Increases with airspeed (proportional to V²).
  • Induced drag. The byproduct of producing lift — energy lost to wingtip vortices and downwash. Decreases with airspeed at constant lift (inversely proportional to V²).

Total drag = parasite + induced. The total drag curve is U-shaped, with a minimum at a specific airspeed. That minimum is L/Dmax — best lift-to-drag ratio.

Speeds related to L/Dmax:

  • Best glide (engine out) — flown at L/Dmax for maximum gliding distance
  • Maximum range (props) — flown at or near L/Dmax
  • Vx (best angle of climb) — close to L/Dmax for normally aspirated piston aircraft

FTGU §1.x · §11

Chalk-talk outline: Board order — (1) draw the parasite drag curve (rising). (2) Draw the induced drag curve (falling). (3) Sum them; the minimum is L/Dmax. (4) Mark Vy, Vx, best glide, max range on the same curve. (5) Show how each is a different operating point on the same drag curve. The drag curve is the single most powerful explanatory diagram in primary flight training. Master this and most performance questions become geometry.

Concept 4Angle of attack and the critical angle

Angle of attack (AoA) is the angle between the wing's chord line and the relative airflow. Lift coefficient (CL) increases with AoA up to the critical angle of attack — beyond which airflow over the upper surface separates and lift collapses. FTGU §1.x · §2.1

Verify in FTGU: the typical critical AoA value for general aviation airfoils (commonly cited as 16–18° but varies by airfoil design). Specific values are airfoil-specific.

The wing always stalls at the same angle of attack — regardless of airspeed, weight, attitude, or load factor. Stall speed varies with all of those; stall angle does not. This is the foundational concept of stall awareness.

Common misconception: "The wing stalls when airspeed gets too low." False. The wing stalls when angle of attack exceeds critical. Low airspeed is the most common cause of high AoA in normal flight, but it isn't the cause of the stall itself. A wing can stall at 200 knots in a high-G pull. This distinction matters for accelerated stalls and for stall recovery technique. FTGU §2.1

Stability vs maneuverability is a tradeoff. More stable airplanes resist deviation but also resist control input. Aerobatic aircraft sacrifice stability for maneuverability. Trainers favor stability.

Concept 6Load factor and Va

Load factor is the ratio of lift to weight. In steady level flight, load factor = 1 (1 g). In a 60° banked level turn, load factor = 2 (2 g). Higher load factor means the wing is producing more lift than the weight — and stall speed climbs accordingly. FTGU §1.x · §2.1

Maneuvering speed (Va) is the maximum speed at which full deflection of any single control surface won't exceed the structural limit load factor. Above Va, full control input may exceed structural limits. Below Va, the wing will stall before the airframe breaks.

Va decreases with weight. Counter-intuitive but correct. A lighter airplane has less mass to accelerate, so the same control input produces a higher load factor faster. Published Va values typically come at gross weight. At lower weights, Va is lower — interpolate per POH. This is why your POH's Va table has multiple entries at different weights. Aero engines

The piston engine — four-stroke cycle, fuel and ignition systems, mixture and leaning, turbocharging, engine instruments.

Light aircraft are powered almost universally by reciprocating piston engines. Modern designs (Lycoming, Continental) trace their heritage to engines designed in the 1930s–50s. The technology is mature, well-understood, and almost entirely mechanical. Knowing how it works lets the pilot diagnose abnormalities, manage the engine through its envelope, and respond intelligently to failures. FTGU §3

Concept 1The four-stroke cycle

Each cylinder runs a four-stroke cycle: FTGU §3.1

  1. Intake. Piston moves down, intake valve open, fuel-air mixture drawn in
  2. Compression. Piston moves up, both valves closed, mixture compressed
  3. Power. Mixture ignited near top of stroke, expansion drives piston down
  4. Exhaust. Piston moves up, exhaust valve open, combustion products pushed out

Four cylinders complete one full cycle every two crankshaft revolutions. In a four-cylinder engine, one cylinder is always in a power stroke at any moment. Six- and eight-cylinder engines smooth this further.

Concept 2Configurations

  • Inline. Cylinders in a single line. Used in some early engines, rare today.
  • Horizontally opposed (flat). Cylinders in two banks, opposing each other. Standard for nearly all modern light aircraft. Compact, low frontal area, good cooling.
  • Radial. Cylinders arranged in a circle around the crankshaft. Used in larger piston aircraft historically; rare in modern light aircraft.
  • V-type. Two banks of cylinders in a V. Used in some specialty aircraft.

FTGU §3.1

Concept 3Fuel system — carbureted vs injected

Two main fuel delivery methods: FTGU §3.2

Carburetor. Fuel and air mix in a venturi before entering the intake manifold. Simple, no electric fuel pump required for some installations, susceptible to carb ice (the venturi pressure drop cools the air, condensing moisture; if temp drops below freezing, ice forms in the throat). Carb heat draws air from a heated source to melt the ice.

Fuel injection. Fuel is metered to each cylinder's intake port (or directly into the cylinder) by mechanical or electronic injectors. No carb ice (no venturi). More precise mixture distribution. May suffer induction filter ice in heavy moisture; alternate air provides unfiltered induction air.

Modern light aircraft engines are predominantly injected (Continental IO-360, Lycoming IO-540, etc.). Older airplanes are often carbureted.

Common misconception: "Fuel injection means no induction icing." False. Carb ice specifically (in the venturi) doesn't occur. But the induction filter, intake manifold, and air cooler can still ice up in heavy moisture, especially at certain temperature ranges. Alternate air provides unfiltered induction air bypassing the filter — used for the same purpose carb heat is used in carbureted engines. FTGU §3.2 · POH §3

Concept 4Mixture and leaning

The fuel-air mixture varies with altitude. At sea level, the engine receives full air density; the mixture control sets fuel-to-air ratio. As altitude increases, air density decreases. Without leaning, the mixture becomes progressively richer with climb — same fuel mass, less air. FTGU §3.3

Operationally:

  • Below approximately 5000 ft DA, leaning isn't typically required for normal operations (varies by engine and POH). At full power on takeoff, mixture is rich for cooling.
  • Above approximately 5000 ft DA, leaning is required even at climb power, or the engine runs grossly rich, loses power, and may run rough.
  • At cruise, lean for fuel economy or cylinder temperature management per POH guidance.

FTGU §3.3 · POH §4 cruise charts (aircraft-specific)

EGT (exhaust gas temperature) is the typical indicator for leaning. Peak EGT occurs at the chemically correct fuel-air ratio. Lean of peak (LOP) and rich of peak (ROP) operations have different temperature and economy profiles — the POH governs.

Concept 5Ignition — dual magnetos

Aircraft engines have two independent magneto ignition systems for redundancy. Each magneto fires its own set of spark plugs (typically two per cylinder). Both magnetos firing produces optimum combustion; one alone produces slightly less power but the engine still runs. FTGU §3.4

The mag check during run-up tests each magneto independently. Selecting LEFT cuts the right magneto and fires only on the left plugs (and vice versa). RPM drop is normal; excessive drop indicates a fouled plug, magneto timing issue, or other ignition problem. FTGU §3.4 · POH §4 run-up procedure

Verify in FTGU and POH: the typical RPM drop limit (commonly cited as 100–150 RPM with a maximum 50 RPM differential between magnetos) — exact values are aircraft-specific and should be read from the POH.

Magnetos are self-energizing — they don't require battery or alternator power. This is critical: in a total electrical failure, the engine continues to run because the magnetos generate their own ignition voltage. The mag switch is also independent of the master switch, which is why "mags off" requires a separate action.

Concept 6Turbocharging

A turbocharger uses exhaust gas energy to drive a compressor that increases manifold pressure above ambient. Result: the engine produces near-sea-level power at altitude, up to a critical altitude where the turbocharger can no longer compensate. FTGU §3.5

Two types:

  • Normalizing. Turbo is sized to produce sea-level manifold pressure at altitude. No boost above ambient at sea level.
  • Boosted (ground-boosted). Turbo can produce above-ambient manifold pressure even at sea level. More performance, but more thermal stress.

Operationally, turbocharged engines require:

  • Smooth throttle handling — sudden changes can over-boost or stall the turbo
  • Cooldown time after high-power operation — to allow turbo bearings to cool before shutdown
  • Awareness of critical altitude — above critical altitude, manifold pressure declines normally with altitude

Propellers

Constant-speed governing, feathering, prop-related forces, multi-engine prop direction.

Concept 1Blade angle vs angle of attack

A propeller is a wing that rotates instead of translating. Each blade has an airfoil cross-section. Two key angles: FTGU §3.7

  • Blade angle — the angle between the blade chord and the rotation plane. Mechanical property of the prop.
  • Angle of attack — the angle between the blade chord and the relative airflow. Depends on blade angle AND aircraft forward speed.

At higher forward speeds, the relative airflow comes at a flatter angle — reducing AoA. To keep the prop producing thrust efficiently, blade angle must increase with forward speed. This is why constant-speed propellers exist.

Concept 2Constant-speed governor

A constant-speed propeller maintains a selected RPM by varying blade angle. The governor mechanism: FTGU §3.7

  • Pilot selects RPM via prop control
  • Governor compares actual RPM to selected RPM
  • If actual > selected: governor coarsens blade angle (more drag, more load, RPM decreases)
  • If actual < selected: governor finens blade angle (less drag, less load, RPM increases)

The governor uses engine oil pressure to drive the prop hub mechanism. The exact direction (oil to coarse vs oil to fine) is type-specific.

Concept 3Oil pressure direction by prop type

Two prop designs differ in how oil pressure drives blade angle: FTGU §3.7 · POH §7 (aircraft-specific)

Prop typeOil pressure drives towardLoss of oil pressure = blades go to

FTGU §3.7 · POH §7 · prop manufacturer's documentation

The twin design is intentional: a dead engine with no oil pressure should not leave a windmilling propeller producing massive drag. Loss of oil pressure on a twin prop tends to feather, removing drag and allowing controlled single-engine flight. This is the opposite of the single-engine case. FTGU §3.7

Don't carry single-engine intuition into twin operations. A pilot who learned in singles and remembers "loss of oil pressure → fine pitch" carries that into twin training and gets it backwards. The mechanical reasoning differs: twins are designed so that loss of oil pressure produces the safer outcome for that aircraft type.

Concept 4Feathering

Feathering rotates the blades parallel to the airstream — blade angle approximately 90° to the rotation plane. A feathered prop produces minimum drag and stops rotating (or rotates very slowly). This is essential after engine failure in a multi-engine airplane: an unfeathered windmilling prop produces enormous drag, more than gear or full flaps. FTGU §3.7 · POH §3

Feathering takes a few seconds (specific time is aircraft-specific — verify in POH). A centrifugal lock pin typically prevents feather below a specific RPM (Seneca II is 800 RPM per POH §7). The lock prevents the prop from feathering every shutdown on the ground. In flight, you must commit to feather BEFORE RPM bleeds below the lock-pin threshold, or feathering becomes mechanically impossible. POH §3 · §7 (aircraft-specific lockout RPM)

Ask the student: "Why does a tailwheel airplane swing dramatically when you raise the tail on takeoff?" (Answer: gyroscopic precession. The prop is a gyroscope. Pushing the tail up applies a forward force at the top of the rotating prop disc. Precession produces a 90°-later force — causing yaw. Most tailwheel pilots learn to anticipate this with rudder. Tricycle gear airplanes don't have this problem because the tail doesn't move during takeoff. This is one of those gyroscopic-precession effects that's invisible until you fly tailwheel.)

Aircraft systems

Electrical, hydraulic, pneumatic, pitot-static. The supporting systems that make complex aircraft work.

Modern airplanes are integrated systems: a primary energy source (engine), distribution networks (electrical buses, hydraulic plumbing, pneumatic lines), and consumers (instruments, controls, lights, environmental systems). Failures cascade across these systems. This section covers the general patterns; airplane-specific details live in the POH §7. FTGU §2.2 · POH §7

Concept 1Electrical system

A typical light-aircraft DC electrical system has: FTGU §2.2 · POH §7

  • Battery. Lead-acid (most common) or sometimes lithium. Provides starting current and reserve power if alternators fail.
  • Alternator(s). Engine-driven generators that produce AC, rectified to DC for the bus.
  • Voltage regulator. Maintains bus voltage at the regulated value (commonly 14V or 28V DC) regardless of alternator RPM or load.
  • Bus. The distribution network that supplies all electrical consumers.
  • Circuit breakers. Protect each circuit. Pop on overcurrent. Can be reset once if cause is unknown — repeated trips indicate a real problem.
  • Master switch. Disconnects the alternator field circuit and / or the battery from the bus. Different airplanes wire this differently.

FTGU §2.2 · POH §7

In multi-engine aircraft, each engine typically drives its own alternator. Both alternators feed a common bus. A single battery serves the whole airplane. An overvoltage relay on each alternator trips that alternator off line if its regulator fails high. The single-battery design means total alternator failure puts the airplane on battery alone — battery duration becomes the limit on remaining flight time. POH §7 (aircraft-specific)

Total electrical failure on an IFR flight is the worst case: gear may not extend hydraulically (backup gravity extension required), instrument lights, communication, navigation all degrade. See System failures for in-flight handling. FTGU §2.2

Concept 2Hydraulic system

Hydraulic systems use pressurized fluid (commonly MIL-H-5606 mineral-based hydraulic oil) to operate gear, flaps, brakes, and sometimes flight controls in larger aircraft. Basic elements: pump, reservoir, valves, actuators. FTGU §2.2 · POH §7

In light aircraft with hydraulic gear, the system is often a self-contained "power pack" — pump, motor, reservoir, valves all integrated into a single unit. A reversible pump can pressurize either the up-side or down-side of the gear actuator depending on selector position, eliminating the need for two pumps. POH §7

Concept 3Pneumatic system — gyros and ice protection

The pneumatic system in light aircraft serves the gyro instruments (attitude indicator, directional gyro) and, on some airplanes, the deicer boots. Two configurations exist: FTGU §2.2 · POH §7

  • Suction (vacuum) systems. Engine-driven pumps create suction that drives gyro rotors. Most common in light singles.
  • Pressure systems. Engine-driven pumps create positive pressure. Allows operation at higher altitudes where suction is unreliable. Common in turbocharged twins (boots and gyros use pressure).

The Seneca II uses a pressure-pneumatic system per POH §7 — verify in your specific POH for the aircraft you fly.

Stalls & spins ✓ Strict-sourced v2

A wing stalls when angle of attack exceeds critical, regardless of airspeed or attitude. A spin is a yawed stall. Recovery: reduce angle of attack.

Stalls and spins are the leading cause of fatal general aviation accidents. Most stall accidents are not pilots flying slowly and watching the airspeed bleed off — they are pilots pulling at unexpected times. Understanding why a wing stalls, when it stalls, and how it stalls is the difference between recognizing the warning signs and being surprised by them. The concept was introduced in Theory of flight; this section goes deeper into the operational side. FTGU §2.1

Concept 1The two stalls every pilot needs to know

Power-off stall (approach to landing stall). Idle power, gear and full flaps extended, simulating final approach. The airplane decelerates with the nose held up. As angle of attack increases, the wing eventually exceeds critical angle. FTGU §2.1

Power-on stall (departure stall). Climb power, gear up, takeoff flaps. Simulates an aggressive climb-out. The angle of attack is high to begin with; pulling further hits critical AoA quickly. The stall is often more dramatic — high power means asymmetric effects (P-factor, torque, slipstream) are working hard, and the airplane tends to roll off to one side. FTGU §2.1

The departure stall is the more dangerous of the two because it happens at low altitude with the asymmetric-thrust effects active.

Multi-engine power-on stalls are not benign. With both engines at climb power and the nose held up at high angle of attack, an asymmetric stall — one wing dropping before the other — can rapidly evolve into a Vmc roll. The dropping wing creates bank that increases AoA on the down-going wing, while reducing it on the up-going wing. Recovery requires reducing power on the operating engine if the stall is asymmetric, not adding more power. POH stall recovery section · TP 219E

Concept 2The accelerated stall

The stall most pilots accidentally encounter is not slow-flight related — it's load-factor related. A wing always stalls at the same angle of attack, but the airspeed at which that AoA is reached depends on load factor. FTGU §2.1

Stall speed in a turn = wings-level stall speed × √(load factor)

The base-to-final overshoot is the classic accelerated-stall accident. A pilot overshoots final, banks more aggressively to correct, and adds back-pressure to keep the nose up — increasing load factor while in a tight turn. Stall speed climbs faster than expected. The wing stalls in the turn. At pattern altitude, recovery may not be possible.

The lesson: aggressive turns near pattern altitude are accelerated-stall territory. If you've overshot final, the correct correction is shallow bank with more rudder if needed, or a go-around. Not steeper bank. Not back-pressure. FTGU §2.1

Ask the student: "You're at 60° bank, level turn. Your published Vs is 50 KIAS clean. What's your effective stall speed in this turn?" (Answer: 50 × √2 = 50 × 1.41 = 70.5 KIAS. Then: "What if you pull a tighter turn — say 75°?" Answer: 50 × 1.96 = 98 KIAS. Almost double the wings-level stall speed. The student who can do this calculation cold understands accelerated stalls; the one who memorizes the table values without the formula doesn't.)

Concept 3The spin

A spin is a stalled, yawed condition where one wing is more deeply stalled than the other. The "good" wing produces lift; the "bad" wing produces less lift and more drag. The result is a self-sustaining spiral descent with the airplane rotating around its vertical axis at a steep nose-down attitude. FTGU §2.1

For a spin to develop, two conditions must be present at the same time:

  1. The wing is stalled (AoA above critical)
  2. The airplane is yawing

Yaw can come from many sources: rudder input, asymmetric thrust (engine failure in a twin), uncoordinated turn, propeller effects at high power. Combine yaw with stall and a spin develops.

Multi-engine airplanes are placarded against intentional spins in nearly every case. Recovery technique is type-specific and may not be effective at all in some configurations. The Piper Seneca II POH §3 provides spin recovery procedure as worst-case guidance; intentional spins are prohibited. POH §2 limitations · POH §3 spin recovery

Standard spin recovery (PARE)

The widely-taught recovery for primary trainers is the PARE method: FTGU §2.1 (general spin recovery)

  1. P — Power idle
  2. A — Ailerons neutral
  3. R — Rudder full opposite the spin direction
  4. E — Elevator forward to reduce angle of attack

Ailerons neutral because aileron input in a stalled condition can deepen the stall on the down-going wing. Rudder breaks rotation. Elevator forward breaks the stall.

Type-specific recovery overrides PARE. Some airplanes have specific recovery procedures that differ from PARE. Twins may have different recovery actions than singles. Always use the manufacturer's recovery procedure if one is published. PARE is the default for airplanes without published recovery procedures and for primary trainers. FTGU §2.1 · POH (aircraft-specific)

Concept 4Stall warning systems

Most certified airplanes have a stall warning system that alerts the pilot before actual stall. FTGU §2.1 · POH (aircraft-specific)

Verify in FTGU: the typical warning margin (commonly cited as 5–10 KIAS before stall, varies by aircraft and certification). The exact margin for any specific airplane is in its POH.

Three common types:

  • Reed-type warning horn. A small valve on the wing leading edge opens at high AoA, allowing airflow through a reed that produces an audible warning.
  • Stall vane (lift detector). Small vane on the wing leading edge pivots up at high AoA, closing an electrical contact that triggers a warning.
  • Angle-of-attack indicator. Direct measurement of AoA, displayed on a dedicated instrument. More common in newer aircraft.

The warning is a margin, not a stall — when it sounds, the wing is approaching critical AoA but not yet stalled. The correct response is to reduce angle of attack immediately. In multi-engine aircraft with heated lift detectors, a heated vane prevents ice-induced false signals or no signal.

Asymmetric thrust & Vmc

The defining problem of multi-engine flight. Why one engine produces yaw the other cannot replace, and the airspeed below which the rudder cannot save you.

A multi-engine aircraft with one engine inoperative produces asymmetric thrust — power on one side, drag on the other. The result is a yawing moment that must be countered by rudder. Vmc is the lowest airspeed at which directional control can be maintained. Below Vmc, the rudder runs out of authority, the airplane yaws toward the dead engine, rolls in the same direction, and may enter an uncontrolled descent or spin. FTGU §2.1 (Vmc) · TP 219E · POH §2 limitations

Concept 1The certification definition of Vmc

Vmca is defined under the certification standards (CAR Standard 523.149, equivalent to FAR 23.149) as the minimum flight speed at which directional control can be maintained, with maximum rudder deflection and not more than 5° of bank, following sudden failure of the critical engine. CAR Std 523.149 · TP 219E definitions

The certification test conditions:

  • All engines developing maximum rated power at the time of failure
  • Aircraft at minimum practical test weight with the most rearward CG
  • Landing gear retracted
  • Flaps in takeoff position
  • The propeller of the failed critical engine windmilling (not feathered)
  • Bank not exceeding 5° toward the operating engine
  • Rudder force not exceeding the certification maximum

CAR Std 523.149 · verify exact rudder force limit in current standard

This is worst case. Real-world Vmc in your airplane today, in this configuration, at this weight, may be lower — but it can also be higher in ways the certification doesn't model.

Concept 3The critical engine and counter-rotation

In a same-rotation twin (both engines rotating clockwise), one engine's failure has a worse effect on Vmc than the other's. The critical engine is the one whose failure most adversely affects performance and handling. In a same-rotation American twin, this is the left engine. FTGU §3.8 · TP 219E

Why the left engine is critical: P-factor. At positive angles of attack, the descending blade of each propeller produces more thrust than the ascending blade. On a clockwise-rotating prop (viewed from cockpit), the descending blade is on the right side. So each engine's thrust vector is offset to the right of its propeller hub.

  • If the right engine fails: thrust on the left is offset right of the left prop centre. The arm from CG to that thrust vector is short. Yaw moment is small.
  • If the left engine fails: thrust on the right is offset right of the right prop centre. The arm from CG to that thrust vector is long. Yaw moment is large.

So losing the left engine produces a larger yaw moment, requiring more rudder, raising Vmc. The left engine is critical in same-rotation twins.

Counter-rotating twins (Seneca II, some Piper and Cessna twins) have engines that rotate in opposite directions. Descending blades are closer to airplane centerline on each engine, so thrust vectors from each engine are symmetric. Neither engine has a longer yaw arm than the other. There is no critical engine. FTGU §3.8

Counter-rotation is one of the design choices that made the Seneca II a popular IFR trainer. The principle scales — twin turboprops and jets typically use either counter-rotating propellers or symmetric thrust arrangements (centerline-thrust pushers) to manage the critical-engine problem. FTGU §3.8

Concept 4The turbocharged-twin altitude paradox

This is the Vmc trap that catches pilots transitioning from normally-aspirated to turbocharged twins. FTGU §3.x · POH performance section

In a normally aspirated twin, engine power decreases with altitude. By a certain altitude, asymmetric thrust isn't enough to overpower the rudder — Vmc drops below stall speed. At higher altitudes, the airplane will stall before it loses directional control.

In a turbocharged twin, the engine maintains rated power up to critical altitude. Asymmetric thrust does not decrease with altitude. Vmc stays roughly constant (or even rises slightly due to thinner air giving the rudder less authority). POH §4 (turbocharged engine performance) · FTGU §3.x

The dangerous outcome: as the airplane climbs, stall speed (in IAS) stays roughly constant, but Vmc may stay constant or rise. There is an altitude where Vmc and stall speed converge. At that altitude, loss of directional control happens at the same instant as the stall — and a spin can result. A spin in a light twin at altitude is a worst-case outcome.

This is why Vmc demonstrations in turbocharged twins use reduced power on one engine (rather than full takeoff power) and are performed above a specified AGL altitude. The procedure trades the worst-case full-power Vmc for a controllable approach-to-Vmc demonstration. POH §4 Vmc demo procedure (aircraft-specific) · TP 219E

Concept 5Recovery from Vmc

If the airplane is approaching Vmc — directional control degrading, more rudder required to hold heading, possibly bank developing toward the dead engine — the recovery is unambiguous and time-critical. FTGU §2.1 · POH §3

  1. Reduce power on the operating engine immediately. The only action that addresses the cause — asymmetric thrust. More rudder won't help; it's already at its limit.
  2. Lower the nose. Trade altitude for airspeed. Get back above Vmca, ideally above Vsse, before re-applying power.
  3. Recover wings level, then reapply power smoothly. Sudden full power risks re-entering the same condition.

The wrong instinct is to pull, bank harder, and try to hold altitude. All three make it worse.

The decision matrix at Vmc: altitude and airspeed before everything else. If at low altitude approaching Vmc, you have very little vertical room. Reduce power, lower the nose, accept the descent. A controlled gear-down landing in a field is survivable. A Vmc roll into the ground is not. FTGU §11 · TP 219E
Chalk-talk outline: Board order — (1) draw airplane top-down with both engines running, vectors balanced. (2) Stop one engine. Show the asymmetric thrust vector and the rudder vector that must counter it. (3) Slow the airplane below Vmc. Show rudder reaches max deflection but yaw moment exceeds rudder authority. (4) Add 5° bank toward operating engine — show horizontal lift component helping the rudder. (5) Now show two failure modes: holding altitude (airplane decelerates further, Vmc grows worse) vs reducing power (yaw moment drops, Vmc falls below current speed, recovered). The geometry tells the story; without the geometry, Vmc recovery is just a memorized list.

Concept 1Electrical failures

Three operationally relevant electrical failure modes:

Single alternator failure (twin)

Dual alternator failure (twin)

Both alternators are off line. The airplane is now on battery only. Battery duration depends on loads and is aircraft-specific (consult POH §3 / §7 for your airplane).

The compass error trap. The magnetic compass is calibrated (compensated) for the airplane's normal electrical environment. In single-engine aircraft, alternator failure means battery only. Times are typically shorter than twins because the battery is sized smaller. Same principle: shed loads, plan to land within the available time. POH §3 (aircraft-specific)

Concept 2Pneumatic failures — gyros and boots

Pneumatic failures degrade the gyro instruments (attitude indicator, directional gyro) and any pneumatic-driven ice protection (deicer boots). FTGU §2.2 · POH §3 · §7

Single pump failure (twin)

One engine-driven pump fails. The other pump carries both gyros through the check-valve manifold. A flag appears on the gauge indicating single-pump operation. Boots on the failed-pump side may operate degraded.

  • Electric turn coordinator (independent of pneumatic system)
  • Magnetic compass for heading
  • Pitot-static instruments (airspeed, altimeter, VSI) for pitch and altitude

Concept 2The restoration drill (twin)

Before securing an engine, attempt restoration. The drill is type-specific but follows a universal logic — test each likely cause in sequence. The Seneca II POH §3.7 illustrates the principle (consult your aircraft's POH for the exact sequence): POH §3 (aircraft-specific) · principle from FTGU §11

  1. Mixture — as required (tests fuel/air ratio)
  2. Fuel selector — CROSSFEED (tests on-side fuel supply)
  3. Magnetos — left or right only (tests ignition)
  4. Alternate air — ON (tests induction blockage)
  5. Aux fuel pump — HI · OFF if no help (tests engine-driven pump)

Each item tests one possible cause. If none restore power, the engine isn't coming back through pilot action. Commit to feather before RPM falls below the lock-pin threshold (the threshold is aircraft-specific — Seneca II is 800 RPM per POH).

Concept 3Engine fire

  1. Fuel selector — OFF (cuts fuel feeding the fire)
  2. Throttle — close (reduces airflow into the engine)
  3. Propeller — FEATHER (stops windmilling)
  4. Mixture — idle cut-off (eliminates residual fuel)
  5. Heater — OFF (the combustion heater runs on the same fuel system)
  6. Defroster — OFF (associated with the heater)
  7. If fire continues — land if terrain permits

Each step removes one element of the fire triangle: fuel (selector + mixture), air (throttle), or heat (feathering stops combustion-related rotation). The heater and defroster come off because the heater is itself a combustion device on the same fuel system. POH §3 (aircraft-specific) · FTGU §10.6

  • At or above abort floor: pilot decision. Adequate runway → close throttles, land. Inadequate runway → continue, identify, feather, retract gear, climb at Vyse, declare emergency, return for landing.

The abort floor is the conceptual ancestor of V1 in jets — the latest decision speed at which abort is still safe.

The most dangerous failure mode is engine failure during the rotation/initial-climb phase with a flap setting that has the airplane briefly below Vmc (e.g., short-field takeoff configurations). If the engine fails here, retard the operating throttle and lower the nose immediately. Maintaining control takes priority over feathering or gear retraction. The atmosphere is the medium aircraft fly through. Concept 2The International Standard Atmosphere

The ISA is a model atmosphere used as a reference for aircraft performance, instrument calibration, and meteorological calculations. It's not what the atmosphere is — it's what the atmosphere is compared to. FTGU §6.1

ISA at sea level:

  • Pressure: 29.92 inches Hg (1013.25 hPa)
  • Temperature: 15°C (59°F)
  • Lapse rate: temperature decreases 2°C per 1000 ft (1.98°C/1000 ft) up to the tropopause

Real-world conditions deviate from ISA. "ISA +15" means temperature is 15°C warmer than standard at that altitude. Higher temperature means lower density, which affects performance, engine output, and true airspeed.

  • Density determines lift, drag, engine output, prop thrust, true airspeed. See Weight, balance, performance.
  • Moisture content drives clouds, precipitation, fog, icing, and thunderstorms. See Clouds & fronts.
  • Vertical motion drives turbulence and convective development. See Turbulence.

Density altitude is the altitude in the standard atmosphere at which the air density would equal the actual air density at your current location. It accounts for both pressure and temperature deviations from standard. FTGU §6.5

The relationship between environmental and adiabatic lapse rates determines stability:

  • Stable atmosphere: environmental lapse rate is less than adiabatic. A rising parcel cools faster than its surroundings, becomes denser, and sinks back. Air resists vertical motion. Smooth flying, layered clouds (stratus), poor visibility in moisture, ice crystals in suspension.
  • Unstable atmosphere: environmental lapse rate is greater than adiabatic. A rising parcel stays warmer than surroundings and continues rising. Air supports vertical motion. Turbulent flying, cumulus and cumulonimbus development, good visibility between clouds, showers and thunderstorms.
  • Conditionally unstable: stable for unsaturated air, unstable for saturated air. If forced up enough to saturate (by a front or terrain), it becomes unstable and continues to rise on its own.

Concept 5Temperature inversions

Normally temperature decreases with altitude. An inversion is a layer where temperature increases with altitude. Inversions are extremely stable — they suppress vertical motion, trapping pollution, moisture, and turbulence below them. FTGU §6.5

Inversion types:

  • Surface (radiation) inversion. Forms on clear, calm nights as the ground radiates heat to space, cooling faster than the air above. Burns off when the sun heats the surface.
  • Frontal inversion. Warm air rides over cold air at a warm front.
  • Subsidence inversion. Air aloft sinking and warming adiabatically meets cooler air below. Typical of high-pressure systems.

FTGU §6.5 · AIM MET 1.1

Inversions matter because they trap moisture and pollution (poor visibility, fog, smog), and are associated with low-level wind shear when surface friction-affected wind meets the geostrophic wind aloft.

Wind

What drives wind, how surface differs from aloft, low-level wind shear, jet streams.

Concept 1What drives wind

Wind is air moving from higher pressure to lower pressure. Three forces shape it: FTGU §6.4

  1. Pressure gradient force. The basic driver. Air accelerates from high to low. Tighter gradient = stronger wind.
  2. Coriolis force. Apparent deflection from Earth's rotation. In the Northern Hemisphere, deflects moving air to the right of its direction of motion. Result: wind doesn't flow directly from high to low — it flows along the isobars, with low pressure on the left.
  3. Friction. Below a certain altitude (the friction layer), surface friction slows wind and weakens Coriolis deflection. Wind near the surface flows at an angle across the isobars, from high to low. Above the friction layer, wind flows along the isobars (geostrophic flow).

FTGU §6.4

Verify in FTGU §6.4: the friction-layer thickness (commonly cited as ~2000 ft AGL) and the typical surface-wind backing/speed reduction values. Standard quotes: surface wind backed ~30° and ½ to ⅔ gradient speed over land; ~10–15° backed and closer to gradient speed over water. These are operational rules of thumb — verify the exact figures FTGU uses.

Operationally, surface wind is typically backed and slower compared to wind aloft. As you climb out of the friction layer, wind veers (rotates clockwise) and accelerates.

Ask the student: "ATIS reports surface wind 270 at 10. Winds aloft forecast for 3000 ft is 290 at 25. Consistent with friction-layer effects?" (Answer: yes. Surface wind is backed (270 vs 290) and slower (10 vs 25) — both consistent with the friction layer slowing the wind and weakening Coriolis turning. If you see surface wind veered RIGHT of winds aloft, something unusual is happening — local terrain channeling, sea-breeze influence, frontal passage. Pattern recognition keeps you safe.)

Concept 3Low-level wind shear

Wind shear is a change in wind direction or speed over a short distance. Low-level wind shear (LLWS) within ~2000 ft of the surface is hazardous because aircraft are slow, low, and have limited recovery options. FTGU §6.4 · AIM MET (verify section)

Sources of LLWS:

  • Frontal shear. Sharp wind change at frontal boundary. Cold fronts produce more abrupt shear than warm fronts.
  • Thunderstorm gust front. Outflow boundary from a downdraft. Microbursts produce extreme shear.
  • Temperature inversion shear. Strong gradient flow above a surface inversion produces sudden shear at the top of the inversion layer.
  • Terrain effects. Buildings, terrain features, runways adjacent to obstacles.

FTGU §6.4

An aircraft penetrating a headwind gust gains airspeed temporarily; penetrating a tailwind gust loses airspeed. Microburst penetration combines a headwind on entry, downburst, then tailwind on exit — combined sequence has caused multiple airline accidents.

The IFR-specific shear trap. An IFR pilot intercepting a glideslope from below can get a headwind boost that holds them above the glideslope, then a tailwind cut that drops them through. Recovery requires anticipation: if you suspect shear conditions, fly the approach with extra airspeed and be ready to go around. FTGU §6.4

Concept 4The jet stream

The jet stream is a narrow band of strong wind near the tropopause, oriented roughly west-to-east in mid-latitudes. FTGU §6.12

Verify in FTGU §6.12: typical jet stream speed (commonly cited as 100–300 kt with cores reaching 200+ kt), and the polar vs subtropical jet distinctions and seasonal behavior.

Operationally, jet streams matter for high-altitude flight planning (favorable east-bound, headwind west-bound) and for clear-air turbulence (CAT). CAT is severe turbulence in clear air, typically near the jet stream's edges where wind shear between the high-velocity jet and slower surrounding air produces eddies.

Clouds & fronts

How clouds form, what cloud types tell you, air masses, and the four front types.

Concept 1How clouds form

Clouds form when moist air is cooled to its dew point and water vapor condenses into liquid droplets or ice crystals around microscopic particles (condensation nuclei). FTGU §6.2

Four mechanisms cool air enough to form clouds:

  1. Convective lifting. Surface heating produces rising air parcels. Cumulus clouds form where rising air reaches its dew point.
  2. Frontal lifting. Warm air pushed up over cold air at a front. Produces extensive cloud systems.
  3. Orographic lifting. Air forced up over mountains or hills. Clouds on windward side; lee side often has descending dry air (rain shadow).
  4. Radiation cooling. Surface cools at night, cooling the air above. If air reaches dew point, fog or low stratus forms. See Fog.

FTGU §6.2

Concept 2Cloud classification

Concept 4Fronts

A front is the boundary between two air masses with different temperature and humidity. Four types: FTGU §6.7

Cold front

A colder, denser air mass advances and undercuts warmer air. Steep frontal slope forces rapid lift of warm air.

  • Narrow band of severe weather along the front
  • Cumuliform clouds, often cumulonimbus
  • Heavy showers, thunderstorms, hail possible
  • Strong gusty wind shift at frontal passage (often S/SW to W/NW in NH)
  • Rapid clearing behind the front
  • Drop in temperature and dew point behind

Cold fronts move quickly and produce intense, short-duration weather. The danger is convective — thunderstorms, hail, severe turbulence. FTGU §6.7

Warm front

A warmer, less dense air mass advances over colder air. Shallow frontal slope.

  • Wide band of weather ahead of the surface front
  • Stratiform clouds in sequence: cirrus → cirrostratus → altostratus → nimbostratus → stratus
  • Continuous light to moderate precipitation
  • Steady wind, gradual veer at passage
  • Lower visibility and ceilings, especially in precipitation
  • Icing in cloud system, especially altostratus/nimbostratus layers in winter

Warm fronts move slowly and produce extensive, long-duration weather. The danger is structural icing in cloud layers and reduced visibility/ceiling. FTGU §6.7

Verify in FTGU §6.7: typical movement speeds for cold vs warm fronts (commonly cited as 25–30 kt cold, 10–15 kt warm) and typical band widths (cold ~50–100 nm, warm ~300–600 nm) — these are climatological averages with significant variability.

Stationary front

Neither air mass advances. Weather similar to a slow-moving warm front but persists for days. Often the precursor to wave development. FTGU §6.7

Occluded front

A faster cold front catches up to a slower warm front, lifting the warm air mass off the surface. Combines features of both. Two sub-types depending on relative coldness of the two cold air masses involved. FTGU §6.7

Chalk-talk outline: Board order — (1) draw two air masses meeting. (2) Show frontal slope (steep cold, shallow warm). (3) Above each, draw cloud development that the slope produces. (4) Below each, draw weather pattern (showers/storms cold, continuous precip warm). (5) For occluded: draw cold front catching warm front, warm air mass lifted off surface. The geometry-to-weather connection is what makes fronts memorable. Pure description without the geometry doesn't stick.

Icing

How ice forms, types, what it does to the airplane, and how to get out of it. Written once — referenced from engine, propeller, IFR planning, and approach minima sections.

Ice on an airplane is among the most lethal weather hazards in single- and twin-piston operations. Ice degrades lift, increases drag, increases stall speed, can blanket pitot heads and static ports, can lock control surfaces, and can choke induction systems. Operating in icing without certification for known icing is illegal and dangerous. FTGU §6.10 · AIM MET 2.2 · CAR 602.11

Concept 1How ice forms on an aircraft

Three conditions must be present simultaneously for structural ice: FTGU §6.10

  1. Visible moisture — cloud, rain, drizzle, wet snow. (Dry snow generally won't accrete.)
  2. Air temperature at or below freezing
  3. Aircraft surface temperature at or below freezing — almost always true if OAT is below freezing and the airframe has been cold-soaked

FTGU §6.10

Verify in FTGU §6.10: the temperature band most associated with structural icing (commonly cited as 0°C down to about −20°C, with most accretion 0°C to −15°C). Below approximately −20°C, supercooled liquid water becomes rare; moisture is more often ice crystals that don't adhere to the airframe.

Concept 3What ice does to the airplane

  • Lift decreases. Ice on the leading edge disrupts laminar flow.
  • Drag increases. Ice changes the airfoil shape, increases parasite drag.
  • Stall speed increases. POH-specified penalty varies by aircraft. Aircraft-specific value for the Seneca II is in its POH §3 (consult the type-specific reference).
  • Stall behavior changes. Ice on the wing can cause stall at lower (less nose-up) angles of attack with reduced or no warning. The airplane may stall an aileron first, producing roll without conventional warning.
  • Weight increases. Heavy clear ice can add significant weight to a light aircraft.
  • Control surface effectiveness degrades. Ice on tail surfaces is particularly dangerous — tailplane stall can produce sudden nose-down pitch unrecoverable at low altitude.
  • Engine performance degrades. Induction ice (carb ice or filter blockage) reduces engine power. See Aero engines.
  • Pitot/static instruments may freeze. See Aircraft systems.

FTGU §6.10 · POH performance/limitations sections (aircraft-specific)

Source note on stall speed penalty: Older training material commonly cites "30%+ reduction in CL,max with even small ice accumulation" for general aviation. The actual penalty depends on airfoil and ice type. Don't quote a specific percentage; instead, treat any visible ice as significant performance degradation and follow POH guidance for icing-specific speeds.

Concept 4Freezing rain and freezing drizzle

Two specific phenomena produce the most dangerous icing: FTGU §6.10 · AIM MET 2.2

Freezing rain (FZRA). Rain falls from a warm layer aloft into a freezing layer below. Droplets are large and at temperatures very close to freezing. Contact with a cold airframe produces clear ice rapidly — often more than ice protection systems can shed. Even ice-protected aircraft cannot operate continuously in heavy freezing rain.

Freezing drizzle (FZDZ). Smaller droplets at similar temperatures. Same risks as FZRA. SLD (supercooled large droplets) are often outside ice protection certification basis.

The vertical structure that produces FZRA: a warm layer above 0°C atop a cold layer below 0°C. Visible on a temperature sounding as a "warm nose."

Freezing rain demands immediate action. Climb above the warm layer (above 0°C) or descend below the freezing layer (back to above 0°C). Do not continue level flight in freezing rain — accumulation rates can exceed any ice protection system, including certified-for-known-icing systems. FTGU §6.10 · AIM MET 2.2

Concept 6The boot-bridging discussion

Older training said "wait until you have at least ¼ to ½ inch of ice before cycling boots, otherwise the boots produce a thinly-iced shape that subsequent ice 'bridges over' and won't shed." Source note: The bridging rule has been the subject of significant industry debate. Many older POHs (including the Seneca II POH) still state wait for ¼ to ½ inch. The POH governs for that aircraft. Concept 7Operational guidance — what to do in icing

Order of priority on entering unforecast icing:

  1. Confirm ice protection systems are operating. Pitot heat, prop deice, windshield heat, boots cycling on ice. Wing ice light on for inspection.
  2. Increase airspeed per POH. Stall speed has increased; flying at the normal cruise margin above clean stall puts you closer to iced stall. POH typically specifies an icing speed adder.
  3. Decide where to go. Climb if cleared and able; descend if climb isn't an option and warmer air is below.
  4. Declare if necessary. ATC will accommodate altitude changes for icing.
  5. Plan to land. Even certified-for-known-icing aircraft are not all-weather aircraft.

Thunderstorms

Three life-cycle stages, three flavors of severity, one rule: don't fly through them.

A thunderstorm is the most violent weather phenomenon a pilot can encounter. Severe turbulence, hail, lightning, downbursts, microbursts, icing, and visibility loss in the same parcel of air. No light aircraft is rated for thunderstorm penetration. The discipline is recognition, avoidance, and route planning. FTGU §6.9 · AIM MET 2.10

Concept 1Required ingredients

Three conditions must be present for thunderstorm formation: FTGU §6.9

  1. Sufficient moisture — high dew points in the lower atmosphere
  2. Unstable lapse rate — environmental lapse rate exceeds saturated adiabatic in mid-levels
  3. Lifting mechanism — convective heating, frontal lifting, orographic lifting, or convergence

FTGU §6.9

Concept 2Life cycle stages

A single-cell thunderstorm progresses through three stages: FTGU §6.9

StageCharacteristics
Cumulus (developing)Strong updraft throughout cloud · cloud building rapidly · no precipitation reaching ground · turbulence inside cloud · tops still growing
MatureUpdrafts AND downdrafts coexist · heavy precipitation · lightning · hail possible · gust front at surface · most violent stage · tops at maximum height (anvil forms)
DissipatingPredominant downdrafts · precipitation diminishing · cell collapses · severe gust front and outflow boundary · still dangerous

FTGU §6.9

Verify in FTGU §6.9: typical stage durations (commonly cited as 15–30 minutes per stage, total cell life 30–90 minutes). These are climatological averages; supercell thunderstorms can persist for hours.

The mature stage is when severe turbulence and lightning are most likely. The developing stage is the only one without precipitation visible from radar — fast-developing TCu can become a thunderstorm before weather radar paints it.

Concept 3Thunderstorm types

  • Air-mass thunderstorms. Form within a single air mass, usually summer afternoon convection. Single-cell, short-lived, easily avoided.
  • Frontal thunderstorms. Form along cold fronts. Can organize into squall lines extending hundreds of miles.
  • Squall lines. Pre-frontal lines of severe thunderstorms ahead of a cold front. Often most violent.
  • Orographic thunderstorms. Mountain-induced. Common in summer afternoons over mountainous terrain.
  • Embedded thunderstorms. Hidden inside extensive cloud cover from a warm front or stable system. Most dangerous because they cannot be visually identified — radar (airborne or ATC) is the only way to see them.

FTGU §6.9

Concept 4Avoidance distances

Standard guidance for thunderstorm avoidance:

Verify in FTGU §6.9 / AIM MET 2.10: exact recommended avoidance distances. Commonly cited values: 20 nm minimum from any storm with tops above 30,000 ft or producing severe turbulence/hail; 10 nm from less severe cells; never fly between two cells less than 40 nm apart. Source documents may use different exact values; use the most conservative published guidance.

  • Avoid the anvil downwind — hail can be thrown out and fall in apparently clear air below
  • Avoid the gust front leading edge — outflow can extend well ahead of the visible cell
  • VFR or IFR flight inside a thunderstorm produces unsurvivable structural loads — don't penetrate, deviate or land

Step 2: Determine VMC / IMC.

Radio navigation

VOR, NDB/ADF, DME, ILS, RNAV/GNSS. How each works, what it gives you, what its limitations are.

Modern instrument navigation rests on a few core systems. VOR and ILS use ground-based VHF/UHF transmissions for bearing and approach guidance. DME measures slant range. NDB/ADF is older LF/MF technology, declining in use. RNAV/GNSS uses GPS satellite constellations for position. Most IFR ops use a combination — typically GNSS as primary with VOR/ILS as backup.

Concept 1VOR — VHF Omnidirectional Range

A VOR transmits a signal that allows aircraft receivers to determine their bearing from the station. Two signals are transmitted on the same frequency: a reference 30 Hz tone and a variable 30 Hz tone whose phase varies with azimuth. The receiver compares the phase difference and determines the radial. FTGU §9.x (radio nav theory)

  • Frequency band: VHF, paired with ILS localizers
  • Range: line-of-sight · service volume varies by station class — verify in AIM
  • Accuracy: on the order of ±2° in good conditions — verify in FTGU/AIM for specific tolerances
  • Limitations: line-of-sight (no signal behind terrain); cone of confusion directly over the station; scalloping in mountainous terrain

FTGU §9.x · AIM COM (verify frequency bands and service volumes)

Verify in current AIM and FTGU: exact VHF frequency band (commonly cited as 108.0–117.95 MHz, but the lower portion is shared with ILS — verify split), and standard service volumes by VOR class (T, L, H — different range expectations).

Concept 2NDB / ADF — Non-Directional Beacon / Automatic Direction Finder

An NDB transmits a non-directional signal in the LF/MF band. The aircraft's ADF receiver determines the bearing TO the station using a directional antenna. FTGU §9.x

  • Frequency band: LF/MF — verify exact range in FTGU/AIM
  • Range: ground-wave propagation · varies with terrain and overwater path
  • NDB-specific errors:
    • Quadrantal error — bearing distortion at certain angles to aircraft heading from internal interference
    • Night effect — sky-wave reflection from ionosphere produces fluctuating bearings, especially at sunrise/sunset
    • Coastline effect — bearing refraction when crossing land/water boundaries at low angles
    • Mountain effect — signal distortion in mountainous terrain
    • Thunderstorm effect — lightning produces interference; ADF needle swings toward the storm

FTGU §9.x (NDB errors)

NDB/ADF is being phased out in much of Canada as RNAV approaches replace it. Some IFR rides still test it; some smaller airports still have only NDB approaches. Maintain proficiency until the system is gone.

Ask the student: "Your ADF needle starts swinging wildly back and forth, then 'points' toward a thunderstorm cell visible to your right. Reliable for navigation?" (Answer: no. ADF responds to lightning RF energy and 'points' to the discharge source. The needle is now indicating storm activity, not the NDB station. Use other navigation. Useful side-effect: the ADF can be a poor-man's stormscope when properly interpreted, but only as supplemental info.)

Concept 3DME — Distance Measuring Equipment

DME measures the slant-range distance from the aircraft to a ground station. The aircraft transmits a query pulse; the station responds; the time delay is converted to distance. FTGU §9.x

  • Frequency: UHF · paired with VOR/ILS frequencies (the pilot tunes the VOR/ILS, the DME automatically uses the paired UHF)
  • Range: line-of-sight
  • Slant range: DME measures slant distance, not horizontal distance · directly over the station, DME reads aircraft altitude in nautical miles, not zero

FTGU §9.x

Verify in FTGU/AIM: exact DME accuracy specifications (commonly cited as ±0.5 nm or 3% of distance, whichever is greater).

Concept 1The pre-flight information requirement

For IFR operations, a complete pre-flight covers:

  • Weather — METARs, TAFs, GFAs, SIGMETs, AIRMETs, freezing levels, winds aloft AIM MET 1.1
  • NOTAMs — for departure, en route, destination, and alternate aerodromes
  • Aerodrome data — runway lengths, lighting, instrument approaches available, fuel availability, customs status (CFS, CAP)
  • Aircraft performance — takeoff distance, climb performance, cruise fuel burn, landing distance (POH)
  • Weight and balance — within envelope at takeoff and at landing (POH + W&B form)
  • Routing — preferred IFR routes, airway/jet route analysis, GNSS suitability
  • Fuel — quantity required by regulation plus operational reserves
  • Alternate — required for IFR; selected based on weather minima

Concept 5Cruise fuel management

Hold entry procedures — the 70°/110° rule

The hold entry depends on the aircraft's heading relative to the inbound leg at the holding fix. Three entries are possible: direct, parallel, teardrop. AIM RAC 9.5

Hold entry sectors — direct (250°-110°), teardrop (180°-250°), parallel (110°-180°)
  • Direct entry. Heading is within ±70° of the inbound course on the holding side. Cross the fix, turn in the direction of the hold, continue around the pattern.
  • Teardrop entry. Heading is within 70° of the inbound course on the non-holding side. Cross the fix, turn 30° away from the inbound course (toward the holding side), fly a teardrop leg, then turn to intercept the inbound course.
  • Parallel entry. Heading is between 70° and 110° of the inbound course on the non-holding side. Cross the fix, parallel the inbound course in the opposite direction, fly outbound, then turn more than 180° to intercept the inbound course.

Wind correction in the hold

The objective is a 1-minute inbound leg. Outbound is adjusted to compensate.

  • Headwind on inbound: shorten outbound (less than 1 minute) so inbound = 1 minute
  • Tailwind on inbound: extend outbound (more than 1 minute)
  • Crosswind: apply wind correction angle on inbound · double the WCA on outbound

The double-WCA outbound rule compensates for the shorter outbound leg duration: less time means more drift correction needed per second.

Ask the student: "You're holding right turns. Wind from your right on the inbound leg. Which direction is your outbound WCA — left or right?" (Answer: outbound, the wind is coming from your LEFT relative to the new heading. So your WCA is to the LEFT — opposite of the inbound correction. And it should be approximately double the magnitude. Drawing this on the board catches a lot of pilots who think "same WCA both legs." It isn't.)

Concept 4CDFA — Continuous Descent Final Approach

Missed approach

When the runway isn't in sight at minima, when conditions degrade, when the approach goes wrong.

Concept 1Mandatory missed approach trigger

The mandatory trigger from CAR 602.128 / AIM RAC 9: at MDA or DA/DH, if the required visual reference is not established and maintained, execute the missed approach immediately. CAR 602.128 · AIM RAC 9

Operationally, additional triggers warrant a missed approach:

  • Runway environment lost after initially seen below minima
  • Approach not stabilized at 1000 ft AGL (operator policy)
  • Glideslope or localizer signal lost during a precision approach
  • Wind shear encountered on final
  • Aircraft not configured correctly at the appropriate point
  • ATC instruction to go around
  • Runway not clear (preceding aircraft, vehicle, animal)
  • Pilot judgment that landing is not safe — for any reason

Concept 2Standard missed approach sequence

  1. Apply takeoff or go-around power
  2. Adjust pitch attitude to climb attitude
  3. Retract flaps to takeoff setting (per POH); retract gear after positive rate of climb
  4. Climb to the published missed approach altitude on the published path
  5. Navigate to the missed approach holding fix
  6. Communicate the missed approach to ATC

CAP procedure (aircraft-specific) · POH for power/pitch/configuration

The published missed approach must be flown unless ATC issues an alternative clearance.

Concept 3Single-engine missed approach in a twin

A missed approach on one engine in a piston twin is a high-risk maneuver. The airplane is configured for landing — gear down, possibly flaps extended, slow — at low altitude, in IMC, with one engine producing zero thrust and the other already at full power.

The single-engine climb gradient available may not meet the published missed approach climb gradient. FTGU §11.3 · POH SE performance section · TP 219E

Discipline:

  • If single-engine, complete the approach when reasonable rather than going missed.
  • Configure for go-around capability — flaps not full at 25° or per POH SE final, Vyse + buffer per POH, gear down only when landing assured.
  • If a single-engine missed becomes necessary: power on operating engine to max continuous, retract flaps as POH directs, retract gear after positive rate, accept the climb gradient available, declare the emergency.
  • If unable to climb at the published gradient, do not attempt to fly the published missed approach into terrain. Maintain altitude, accept the descent, navigate visually if able, and accept the consequences of a controlled off-airport landing rather than CFIT.
Ask the student: "You're on a single-engine ILS to a Cat I airport, ceiling 250 ft, breaking out. You're stabilized. The runway environment isn't in sight at DH. What now?" (Answer: published response is missed approach. But the SE missed in a piston twin is high-risk. The better discipline is anticipation — have the destination set up so that breaking out is likely. If it goes missed in IMC on one engine, declare and request immediate vectors to the most accessible runway with the best weather. Concept 4Post-missed-approach options

  1. Hold and wait. Hold at the missed approach holding fix, await weather improvement, attempt the approach again. Each attempt costs fuel and time.
  2. Try a different approach. If a different approach (different runway, different type) might give better minima, request it.
  3. Divert to the alternate. Fly to the planned alternate. The "missed approach + divert" scenario is exactly what the alternate fuel reserve is for.

Cold-temperature corrections

Why the altimeter lies in cold air, when corrections must be applied, how to compute them. The most-overlooked safety item in Canadian winter IFR.

Concept 1Why the altimeter lies in cold air

A pressure altimeter reads true altitude only when the temperature column matches the standard atmosphere. When air is colder than ISA, the altimeter over-reads — true altitude is lower than indicated. CAP GEN · FTGU §6.3 (theory)

The mnemonic: "From high to low, look out below" applies to both pressure (high to low pressure) and temperature (warm to cold). Both effects compound in winter — a cold front bringing colder air and lower pressure produces the worst case.

Concept 2When corrections must be applied — CAP GEN

CAP GEN specifies the rule: cold-temperature corrections are required on published minimum IFR altitudes when the surface temperature at the aerodrome is significantly below standard. CAP GEN p.A4

The general rule:

  • Apply corrections when surface temperature is 0°C or colder at the aerodrome
  • For approaches with MDA/DA 1000 ft HAA or higher, begin corrections at +10°C or colder

CAP GEN p.A4

Segments requiring correction:

  • MSA — Minimum Safe/Sector Altitude
  • TAA — Terminal Arrival Area altitudes
  • Initial approach altitude
  • Intermediate approach altitude
  • Final approach altitude (FAF crossing altitude, MDA/DA)
  • Missed approach altitude (including holding altitude)

What is NOT corrected: ATC-assigned altitudes that the pilot has accepted. ATC has separation responsibility and applies its own corrections. Vectoring altitudes assigned by ATC are temperature-corrected by ATC and require no compensation by the pilot. CAP GEN p.A4

When corrections are applied to a published mandatory altitude or missed approach holding altitude, the pilot must advise ATC of the temperature-corrected altitude prior to crossing the associated waypoint. CAP GEN p.A4

Concept 2VFR-into-IMC

VFR flight into IMC is among the most lethal accident chains in general aviation. The accident sequence: FTGU §11.6

  1. Pilot departs VFR with marginal weather forecast
  2. Weather at destination is worse than forecast (or pilot encounters lowering ceiling en route)
  3. Pilot descends below cloud, ceiling continues lowering, pilot follows the deck down
  4. Pilot enters IMC at low altitude, attempts to maintain VFR by reference to the ground
  5. Spatial disorientation develops; the airplane enters a graveyard spiral or stall/spin
  6. CFIT or loss-of-control accident

The escape from this chain is to turn back at the first sign of deteriorating weather, before commitment to marginal VFR. Once the airplane is in lowering ceiling and reduced visibility, options narrow rapidly.

For pilots who are IFR-rated but on a VFR flight, the option exists to file IFR in the air via Flight Service or directly with ATC. This is permitted at any time and is the right answer when conditions deteriorate. Unrated VFR pilots have no such option; the only escape is to turn back. FTGU §11.6 · AIM RAC 3.x

Ask the student: "You're on a VFR flight, IFR-rated. Weather ahead is deteriorating — ceiling 1500 broken, visibility 4 SM in light rain. The forecast had said 3000 broken, 6 SM. What do you do?" (Answer: file IFR in the air, or divert to a closer field, or turn around. The "press on hoping it clears" answer is the accident chain. Weight, balance, performance

The pre-flight calculations that determine whether the airplane will perform as expected.

Concept 1Weight and balance principles

Two limits matter for every flight: FTGU §11.5 · POH §6 weight and balance

  1. Maximum gross weight. The airplane is structurally certified up to this weight. Exceeding it risks airframe damage and degrades all performance.
  2. Center of gravity (CG) within limits. The CG must be within published forward and aft limits for the actual gross weight. Outside the envelope, the airplane is unstable or uncontrollable.

Weight is the simpler limit — empty weight + fuel + passengers + baggage ≤ max gross. CG requires moment calculations: each load contributes a moment (weight × arm from datum); CG = total moment / total weight.

The CG envelope is shaped:

  • Forward CG limit may move rearward at higher weights (more aft CG permissible at heavier weights due to inherent stability characteristics)
  • Aft CG limit may move forward at higher weights

FTGU §11.5 · POH §6 envelope diagram

The airplane must be inside this envelope at takeoff, throughout the flight (fuel burn shifts CG), and at landing.

Human factors

Vision, vestibular illusions, fatigue, hypoxia, dehydration. Biological and psychological factors affecting performance.

Concept 1Vision

The eye has two types of light receptors: cones (color, fine detail, requires light) and rods (peripheral, low light, no color). Operational implications: FTGU §10.5

  • Day vision uses primarily cones · sharp, color-rich, central focus
  • Night vision uses primarily rods · less sharp, no color, peripheral
  • Night blind spot — the central fovea (cone-dense) becomes a blind spot at low light · objects are seen better with peripheral vision at night · the technique is to scan, not stare
  • Dark adaptation — once dark-adapted, exposure to bright light requires re-adaptation · use red cockpit lighting to preserve night vision when possible

FTGU §10.5

Verify in FTGU §10.5: the typical dark adaptation time (commonly cited as 20–30 minutes for full adaptation).

Visual scanning techniques:

  • Sector scan — divide visible sky into sectors, scan each in sequence with brief stop
  • Saccadic scan — quick movements between fixations · how the eye actually works · staring rarely detects motion
  • Empty-field myopia — at altitude in haze or featureless sky, the eye relaxes to short focus and can miss distant traffic · counteract by deliberately focusing on distant features

  • Alcohol. CAR 602.03 prohibits acting as a crew member within a specified period after consuming alcohol — verify the exact bottle-to-throttle time and BAC limits in current CAR.

Wake turbulence

Wingtip vortices, separation standards, encounter recovery.

Every aircraft producing lift produces wingtip vortices — counter-rotating cylinders of air trailing behind each wingtip. Heavy aircraft produce strong vortices; light aircraft following closely behind can be rolled inverted by the vortex, with potentially fatal consequences at low altitude. FTGU §10.7 · AIM RAC 5.4

Concept 1Why wake exists

A wing producing lift creates higher pressure below the wing and lower pressure above. At the wingtip, high-pressure air below flows around the tip to the lower-pressure region above. The result is a circular flow — a vortex — trailing behind each wingtip. FTGU §10.7 · §1.x (induced drag mechanism)

Vortex strength scales with:

  • Aircraft weight — heavier airplane needs more lift, creating stronger vortices
  • Aircraft slow speed — slow flight requires high angle of attack, producing strong vortices
  • Clean configuration — flaps and gear extended actually reduce vortex strength by spreading the lift production over a wider span

The strongest wake comes from a heavy, slow, clean aircraft — exactly the configuration of a heavy jet on takeoff or landing. This is why wake separation rules apply specifically to that phase of flight.

Concept 2Vortex behavior

Once shed, the vortices descend and drift with the wind. They typically: FTGU §10.7 · AIM RAC 5.4

  • Descend below the flight path of the generating aircraft
  • Drift downwind
  • Persist for some time in calm air; dissipate faster in turbulent air

Verify in FTGU §10.7 / AIM RAC 5.4: exact vortex descent rate (commonly cited as approximately 500 fpm) and persistence time (commonly cited as 1–3 minutes in calm air). These are operational averages; actual values depend on atmospheric conditions.

Operationally:

  • Following a heavy aircraft on landing — fly slightly above their flight path, touch down well beyond their touchdown point
  • Departing behind a heavy aircraft — wait for separation; rotate before their rotation point if possible; climb above their flight path
  • Crosswind on a parallel runway — wake from the upwind runway can drift onto the downwind runway
  • Tailwind landing or takeoff — wake stays on the runway longer, increasing risk for following aircraft

Concept 3ATC separation standards

ATC applies wake separation when light or medium aircraft follow heavy aircraft. Specific separation values are in AIM RAC 5.4. AIM RAC 5.4

Verify in current AIM RAC 5.4: exact separation values (in nautical miles or time), wake category definitions (Super, Heavy, Medium, Light), and any recent changes to category thresholds. Wake separation rules have been revised multiple times to incorporate new aircraft types (A380 introduced "Super" category, etc.).

The pilot can waive these rules by accepting "at your discretion" or "wake turbulence advised, takeoff at your own discretion." If the pilot accepts, the wake separation responsibility transfers to the pilot. This is not a casual decision. AIM RAC 5.4

Concept 4If you encounter wake

If you're rolled by an unexpected wake encounter:

  1. Recover from any unusual attitude — bank and pitch, treat as any other unusual attitude recovery
  2. Climb out of the wake area — descend through the rolling vortex if very close to the ground; climb if at altitude
  3. Report — to ATC and to other traffic. Wake encounters are PIREP-worthy

FTGU §10.7 · AIM RAC 5.4

Severe wake encounters at low altitude are difficult to recover from. Prevention is the only reliable defense — maintain separation, fly above the flight path of preceding heavies, accept ATC's separation when offered.

Decision making

The frameworks for in-flight decisions. When to commit, when to divert, when to declare. Pilot judgment under structured pressure.

Most aviation accidents are not equipment failures — they are decision failures. The accident chain is typically a series of decisions made under increasing pressure, each one acceptable in isolation, that compound into an unsafe situation. The discipline of breaking the chain by recognizing patterns, making the right call early, and accepting the cost of that call is the single most important pilot skill. The frameworks introduced in Airmanship are the structure; this section is the application.

Concept 1The five aviate-navigate-communicate priorities

Under pressure, pilots can become task-saturated. The classic priority structure:

  1. Aviate — fly the airplane. Maintain control, attitude, airspeed, altitude as appropriate to the situation. If the airplane is not under control, nothing else matters.
  2. Navigate — know where you are and where you're going. Maintain orientation, follow the assigned route, deviate as needed for safety.
  3. Communicate — talk to ATC and other traffic. Communication enables coordination but does not substitute for the first two.

The order is non-negotiable under stress. A pilot trying to communicate while the airplane is uncontrolled is making a fatal mistake. Drop the radio, fly the airplane, then tell ATC what you're doing.

Concept 2The cost of indecision

Many aviation accidents involve a long delay before a critical decision. The pilot recognizes the problem, considers options, hopes the situation will improve, and waits — until the situation has degraded past the point where safe options remain.

Examples:

  • The pilot encountering icing who delays descent to warmer air, hoping to break out of the layer, until ice accumulation is critical
  • The pilot in deteriorating weather who delays diverting until the destination is below minima and the alternate has also deteriorated
  • The pilot with a sick engine who delays securing it, hoping to nurse it home, until the secondary failure (fire, oil starvation) commits to a forced landing
  • The pilot continuing an unstable approach hoping to "salvage" the landing rather than going around

The discipline: make the decision when the option exists, not when it is forced on you. Diverting to an alternate while you have fuel and time is a routine maneuver. Diverting because you've burned the reserve is an emergency. The same physical action, vastly different outcomes.

Concept 3Declaring an emergency

Pilots are sometimes reluctant to declare an emergency. Concerns about paperwork, about being judged, about over-reacting to a manageable situation. These concerns are usually misplaced.

Declaring an emergency:

  • Gives the pilot priority handling — clearance changes, direct routing, runway availability
  • Alerts emergency services — they will be ready when you land
  • Clears traffic from your airspace
  • Gives you the authority to deviate from any rule or clearance to the extent necessary for safety (CAR 602.31)
  • Almost never results in adverse action by Transport Canada — TC's interest is in the safety of flight, not in punishing pilots who recognize they need help

The two declaration levels:

  • MAYDAY MAYDAY MAYDAY — distress · life is at risk or aircraft is in immediate danger
  • PAN-PAN PAN-PAN PAN-PAN — urgency · the situation is concerning but not immediately life-threatening

If you're not sure which to use, use the higher one. ATC can de-escalate; you can't always escalate later.

Single-engine handling

The integrated response to engine failure in a multi-engine airplane. The drill, the decisions, the discipline.

A multi-engine airplane with one engine failed is in the worst possible flight configuration: asymmetric thrust, asymmetric drag, reduced performance, increased workload. Three things must be simultaneously true to maintain controlled flight: airspeed above Vmca, bank toward the operating engine, and the dead engine's prop feathered. The drill exists to achieve all three quickly and reliably.

Concept 1The three things that must be true

Lose any one and the airplane is operating below its design margin:

  • Airspeed above Vmca. Below this speed, the rudder cannot overcome asymmetric thrust at full power. See Asymmetric thrust & Vmc.
  • 5° bank toward operating engine. Horizontal lift component offsets yaw. Wings level, Vmc is higher.
  • Dead engine prop feathered. Removes the largest single drag source on the airplane.

The third is the only one that takes time and a deliberate action. Airspeed and bank respond within seconds; feathering takes 6-8 seconds plus the time to identify and commit. Every second the prop windmills is lost climb capability.

Concept 2The integrated drill

  1. Aviate. Maintain control. Rudder against the yaw. Pitch for Vyse. Bank 5° toward the operating side.
  2. Identify. Dead foot, dead engine. Out loud: "left foot dead, left engine dead."
  3. Verify. Retard the throttle of the suspected dead engine. Confirm power loss matches expectation. If retarding causes additional yaw or power loss, push the throttle back up — wrong engine.
  4. Decide. Restart attempt or commit to feather. Below 800 RPM, feather is no longer possible.
  5. Restart attempt (if appropriate): mixture · fuel selector · magnetos · alternate air · aux pump.
  6. Commit to feather. Mixture cut-off, prop feather, throttle close.
  7. Secure. Magnetos off, alternator off, fuel selector off, electrical load reduced.
  8. Trim. 5° bank, ball appropriate to airframe (typically slightly toward operating engine).
  9. Communicate. Declare. ATC clears the airspace.
  10. Plan. Where are you going? Distance to nearest suitable airport. Single-engine performance available. Weather at the divert.

The discipline is in the order. Aviate first. Identify before verifying. Verify before feathering. Feather before securing. Secure before planning. Each step has a reason; the order is the reason. FTGU §11.3 · TP 219E item 25

Emergency flow

The structured response that applies to every emergency, regardless of specifics. A common pattern under pressure.

Every emergency follows roughly the same structural pattern, regardless of the specific failure. The pilot who has internalized this flow handles unfamiliar emergencies more effectively than one who knows individual checklists by rote.

Concept 1The universal flow

  1. Maintain aircraft control. Always first. If the airplane is uncontrolled, nothing else matters.
  2. Analyze the situation. What has failed? What are the consequences? How much time is available?
  3. Take appropriate action. Memory items if required. Reference checklist when time permits.
  4. Land as soon as conditions permit. Not "as soon as the checklist is complete" — as soon as a safe landing is available. Sometimes the analysis says fly the planned route to the original destination; sometimes it says divert immediately; sometimes it says off-airport landing now.

The order — control, analyze, act, land — applies to engine failures, electrical failures, gear problems, fire, smoke, structural concerns. The specifics of step 3 vary; the structure is universal.

Concept 2Memory items vs reference checklists

Some emergencies require immediate action — there is no time to find a checklist. These are the memory items. Engine fire, engine failure on takeoff, smoke in the cockpit, runaway trim, depressurization. The first three to five steps are memorized cold and executed without reference. After the immediate threat is contained, the reference checklist is consulted to complete the procedure.

Other emergencies are slower and less time-critical. Single alternator failure, partial gear extension, mild engine roughness. These can be worked through with the reference checklist from the start.

The discipline: know which is which. A pilot who tries to look up engine-fire procedures during an actual fire is making a fatal mistake. A pilot who flies a complete memory drill for a single alternator failure is wasting attention that could be applied to flying the airplane. FTGU §11.0

Diversion planning

When to divert, where to divert, how to execute. The maneuver that turns a problem into an outcome.

Concept 1Triggers for diversion

Common triggers:

  • Destination weather has degraded below approach minima
  • Alternate weather has degraded — second-tier diversion to a third option
  • Fuel margin has eroded below planned reserve
  • Mechanical issue (sick engine, electrical, hydraulic) makes continuing inadvisable
  • Pilot fatigue or incapacitation
  • Passenger emergency (medical)
  • Weather encountered en route requires a different routing
  • Forecast for arrival window has changed materially

Concept 2Selecting a divert airport

Criteria for divert selection (in priority order):

  1. Within fuel range with reserves intact
  2. Weather suitable — at or above the minimums you can fly
  3. Runway adequate — length, surface, wind
  4. Approach available for the conditions
  5. Services available — fuel, mechanic if needed, customs if international
  6. Familiar — pilot has been there before, or has reviewed the airport

The "familiar" criterion is underrated. A pilot diverting under stress to an unfamiliar airport is task-loaded by chart review, frequency tuning, runway identification, and approach selection. A pilot diverting to a familiar airport is freed to handle the underlying problem. Many experienced pilots maintain a mental list of "if I have to divert from this route, here's where I go" — pre-planned diversions for the routes they fly regularly.

Concept 3Execution

The diversion itself:

  1. Decide. Commit to the divert. Stop debating.
  2. Set up navigation. Direct GPS to the airport, or radial-and-distance to the nearest VOR.
  3. Talk to ATC. Request divert clearance. Provide reason if asked. Most divert requests are approved without question.
  4. Brief the approach. Frequency, course, altitudes, MDA/DA, missed approach. Cold-temp corrections if applicable.
  5. Communicate with the destination. If diverting to a controlled field, ATIS, ATC frequency. If uncontrolled, MF or UNICOM.
  6. Land normally. Treat the diverted approach with the same discipline as a planned approach — stabilized, configured, briefed.

The post-diversion task: refuel, address the original issue, decide whether to continue or end the trip. Many divert situations are not single-leg problems — they're "the day's plans need to change" situations. Don't try to make the original schedule work after a divert. The flight that ends with a safe landing is the successful flight, regardless of where it landed. FTGU §11.6

Integration · scenarios

Cross-system scenarios

Real flight test orals don't ask "what's Vmc." They ask scenarios that force integration across multiple zones of the document. These are the ones to practice.

Scenario · Engine + Ice + Electrical
You're at 9000 ft IFR in cloud, OAT −5°C, picking up moderate ice. Boots cycling, prop deice on, windshield panel on. The left engine MP starts dropping slowly, fuel flow steady. Then an ALT light illuminates on the right side. Walk through your priorities.

Three problems, three priorities. Ice is the immediate killer if not managed. Engine roughness is the medium-term killer. Electrical is the long-term killer. First (within seconds): Confirm ice protection is working — boots cycling, prop deice ammeter in green, windshield panel hot. Second: Address the left engine MP loss. Alternate air ON — induction icing is the most likely cause given the conditions. Check fuel, mixture. Third: Single ALT failure procedure. Cycle the alt switch, reset CB once. Don't shed the windshield heat unless single-alt operation requires it. Fourth: Decision. You're in icing IFR with one engine sick and one alternator down. This is divert territory. Get a clearance, declare if needed, exit the icing layer. The trap: trying to fix all three simultaneously and losing situational awareness. Aviate-navigate-communicate. The airplane is still flying — keep it flying, then troubleshoot one thing at a time.
Scenario · Engine + Approach
You're 8 nm out on an ILS approach, IMC, ceiling 400 OVC, visibility 2 SM in light rain. Cleared down to 2000, glideslope intercept any moment. Right engine fails. Walk through.

The decision is whether to continue the approach or go missed. Aviate first: rudder, identify, verify, feather. Trim 5° toward operating. Maintain Vyse 89. Navigate: single-engine missed approach is the worst-case maneuver in a light twin — at 400 ft AGL with the airplane configured for landing, climbing on one engine through cloud is marginal at best. Decide: continue. An ILS to a runway with a survivable ceiling is better than a single-engine missed approach into deteriorating weather. Configure for single-engine final: 25° flaps, gear down at the right point, Vref 91 KIAS, full flaps only when landing is assured. Communicate: declare the emergency. Don't try to handle this without telling ATC. The principle: in a light twin, the bias is toward landing, not toward going around.
Scenario · Cold weather + Approach
Winter approach to YQT, surface temperature −30°C. Published MDA on the LOC approach is 1500 ft. Field elevation is 653 ft. Walk through your altitude management on this approach.

Surface temp −30°C is well below 0°C, so cold-temperature corrections apply. Calculate: MDA 1500 ft is 847 ft above field elevation. Round up to 1000 ft for the chart. At −30°C, correction at 1000 ft is 160 ft. Corrected MDA: 1660 ft instead of 1500. Brief: "Corrected MDA 1660, advised ATC. Missed approach altitude... apply correction." Same correction logic for missed approach altitude. The trap: Pilot accepts published MDA mentally because "the airport is in sight at 1500." But the obstacles between you and the runway threshold may be at altitudes that the corrected MDA — not the published MDA — was designed to clear. Fly the corrected number. Tell ATC. Document the correction in your approach brief.
Scenario · Cumulative anomalies
Cross-country VFR-on-top, 11,500 ft, 200 nm into a 350 nm leg. Right engine starts running rough — 100 RPM lower than left, slight EGT split. Then 10 min later you notice the left fuel quantity has dropped faster than expected. You've been on auto-rich the whole time. What's going on, and what do you do?

The diagnosis comes from connecting the dots. Right engine roughness with EGT split suggests a single-cylinder problem — possibly fouled plug, intermittent ignition, partial fuel-injector blockage. Left fuel dropping faster suggests... most likely scenario: the right engine has an issue causing it to run rich, and you've been subconsciously adding power on the right to match the left, burning more fuel per unit time on that side. Auto-rich at 11,500 wastes fuel. Action: Lean appropriately for altitude. Mag check on the right engine. Alternate air briefly. If roughness clears, continue with awareness. If not, decide whether to continue or divert. The integration lesson: piston twins fail gradually more often than catastrophically. Pattern recognition — small anomalies that, taken together, point to a single cause — is the pilot's job.

Oral question bank

Common multi-engine and IFR oral questions, structured with the answers that demonstrate complete system understanding.

The Canadian flight test rubric (TP 219E for multi, TP 9939E for IFR) rewards complete answers — not memorized facts but demonstrated understanding. The questions below are common across both rides; the answers below the questions are structured to show the integration the examiner is looking for.

Theory · multi-engineVmc questions

Q: Define Vmc.

A: Vmca is the minimum airspeed at which directional control of the airplane can be maintained with the critical engine windmilling, the operating engine at takeoff power, max-rearward CG, certification weight, takeoff configuration, and bank not exceeding 5° toward the operating engine. The certification standard is FAR 23.149 and CAR Standard 523.149.

Q: List the factors that affect Vmc.

A: Power on operating engine (more power = higher Vmc), bank toward operating engine (more bank = lower Vmc), weight (heavier = lower Vmc), CG (forward = lower Vmc), configuration (drag = lower Vmc), prop state of inop engine (windmilling = higher Vmc, feathered = lower), and density altitude (with the turbocharged-twin exception). Temperature is via density altitude.

Q: How does Vmc behavior differ between turbocharged and normally-aspirated twins?

A: In normally-aspirated twins, Vmc decreases with altitude because engine power decreases. In turbocharged twins, the engine maintains rated power up to critical altitude, so Vmc doesn't decrease with altitude. The dangerous altitude is where Vmc and stall speed converge — a stall at Vmc can become a spin. This is why the Seneca II Vmc demo in POH §4.49 uses reduced power and is performed above 3500 ft AGL.

Q: What's the recovery procedure if you find yourself approaching Vmc?

A: Reduce power on the operating engine immediately. Lower the nose to gain airspeed. Recover wings level, then reapply power smoothly when above Vsse. The wrong instinct is more rudder, more bank, or holding altitude — all make it worse.

Operations · IFRAlternate weather questions

Q: What are the IFR alternate weather requirements?

A: Per CAR 602.122 and AIM RAC 3.13: the alternate must have, at expected arrival time, ceiling and ground visibility at or above the published alternate minima. With two or more usable precision approaches to separate runways: ceiling 400 ft (or DH+200 if higher), visibility 1 SM (or published+1 if higher). With one precision approach: 600 ft / 2 SM. With one non-precision approach: 800 ft / 2 SM.

Q: How do you handle TAF terms BECMG, TEMPO, and PROB for alternate qualification?

A: BECMG conditions apply at the end of the BECMG period — those conditions must meet alternate minima. TEMPO conditions during the TEMPO period must meet alternate minima — if even one TEMPO group is below mins, the alternate doesn't qualify for that period. PROB conditions: the worse condition must meet alternate minima.

Q: What's the IFR fuel requirement for a propeller-driven airplane?

A: Per CAR 602.121: with an alternate filed, fuel to fly to the destination, execute approach and missed approach there, fly to the alternate, land, and fly 45 minutes at normal cruise speed. Without an alternate (rare), fuel for destination + missed + 45 minutes. Operationally, prudent margin above the regulatory minimum is standard practice.

Operations · IFRApproach and missed approach

Q: When must cold-temperature corrections be applied?

A: Per CAP GEN: when surface temperature at the aerodrome is 0°C or colder, or for approaches with MDA/DA 1000 ft HAA or higher, beginning at +10°C or colder. Apply to MSA, TAA, initial/intermediate/final approach altitudes including MDA/DA, and missed approach holding altitudes. Do not apply to ATC-assigned altitudes that you've accepted (ATC is responsible for those). Advise ATC of corrected mandatory altitudes before crossing the associated waypoint.

Q: What's the difference between MDA and DA?

A: MDA (Minimum Descent Altitude) is the floor of a non-precision approach — the airplane descends to MDA and holds, then either lands if visual or executes missed approach at the MAP. DA (Decision Altitude) is the point on a precision or APV approach where the decision is made — at DA, if visual reference is established, continue; if not, immediate missed approach. MDA is a hold-and-look altitude; DA is a decision-point altitude.

Q: What's CDFA?

A: Continuous Descent Final Approach. The recommended technique for non-precision approaches: instead of "dive and drive" (descend rapidly to MDA, level off, fly until MAP), descend at a constant rate from FAF to a derived Decision Altitude that's MDA + a small visual segment buffer. If visual at DA, continue and land; if not, immediate missed approach. CDFA is more stabilized, reduces CFIT risk, and is recommended in AIM RAC 9.16.

Operations · communicationsLost comms

Q: Walk me through lost-comm procedures under IFR.

A: Squawk 7600. Determine VMC/IMC. If in VMC, remain VMC and land as soon as practicable. If in IMC, continue IFR. Route: fly the route assigned in the last clearance, or vector route, or expected route, or filed route — in that priority order. Altitude: fly the highest of last assigned, MEA/MOCA for the segment, or expected altitude. Begin descent and approach at flight-plan ETA. Per CAR 602.137 and AIM COM 4.2.

Theory · weatherIcing questions

Q: What conditions are required for structural icing?

A: Three conditions must be present: visible moisture, OAT at or below 0°C, and aircraft surface temperature at or below 0°C. The dangerous band is 0°C to about −15°C, where supercooled liquid water is most abundant. Below −20°C, moisture is mostly ice crystals that don't accrete.

Q: What's the worst type of ice and why?

A: Clear (glaze) ice. Large supercooled droplets flow back along the surface before freezing, producing a heavy clear coating that destroys the airfoil contour and can flow back of de-ice boots into unprotected areas. Forms in cumuliform clouds and freezing rain at warmer subzero temperatures. Worse than rime because it spreads beyond the leading edges and is harder for boots to shed.

Q: What do you do if you encounter freezing rain?

A: Climb above the warm layer above the freezing layer (above 0°C), or descend below the freezing layer (back to above 0°C). Do not continue level flight in freezing rain — accumulation rates can exceed any ice protection system, including certified-for-known-icing systems. Declare if necessary. ATC will accommodate altitude changes for icing.

Memory items framework

Criteria for a memory item

An action belongs in memory if all three are true:

  1. The situation requires immediate action (no time to find a checklist)
  2. The wrong action will significantly worsen the situation
  3. The first 3-5 steps cover the immediate threat

Typical multi-engine memory items

  • Engine failure on takeoff (above and below abort floor)
  • Engine failure in flight (below Vmca)
  • Engine fire
  • Smoke or fire in cockpit
  • Runaway trim
  • Door open in flight
  • Restart attempt drill
  • Engine securing (feather) drill

The drill discipline

Memory items are drilled. Pilots who can recite their memory items but haven't physically rehearsed them are not ready. Sit in the airplane on the ground (or in a sim), say the trigger condition out loud, then perform the actions on the actual controls. Repeat until the actions are automatic. The goal: when the real situation occurs, hands move before conscious thought.

Module 01 · Powerplant Group

Powerplant

Engine, turbocharger, induction, fuel, oil, propellers, governors — and the single-engine handling that depends on all of them working together.

Two Continental TSIO-360 engines drive this airplane. They are counter-rotating — left rotates clockwise, right counter-clockwise, viewed from the cockpit — which eliminates the critical-engine concept. Each engine is a self-contained system with its own fuel path, oil sump, alternator, instrument-air pump, and propeller governor. But the components within each engine are tightly interlocked: fuel needs the engine to spin its pump, the pump needs combustion, combustion needs air through the turbo and metered fuel from injection, oil pressure controls propeller pitch, and the propeller is what turns all of it back into thrust. Pull any one thread and the whole system shifts. That's why we study them as a group.

Components Six interlocking subsystems

Engine
Continental TSIO-360E/EB (left, clockwise rotation) and LTSIO-360E/EB (right, counter-clockwise). Six-cylinder, horizontally opposed, air-cooled, fuel-injected, turbocharged.
  • 200 hp at sea level / 215 hp at 12,000 ft
  • 2575 RPM max · 7.5:1 compression · 360 in³ displacement
  • Counter-rotation eliminates critical engine
  • Dynafocal mounts · removable cowl panels for inspection
Turbocharger
Ray-Jay exhaust-driven. Compressor pressurizes induction air. No automatic boost controller — the throttle is your only boost limit at low altitude.
  • 40" Hg manual ceiling · 42" Hg pressure relief valve
  • Fixed bypass orifice preset for 40" at 12,000 ft DA
  • Overboost annunciator illuminates ~39.8" Hg
  • Smooth throttle movement only — let turbo speed stabilize
Induction & Alternate Air
Primary path is filtered cowl air. Alternate air bypasses the filter and draws unfiltered, heated air from inside the cowling. A suck-in door auto-opens if the primary blocks.
  • Alt air ON: induction icing, blocked filter, restart attempts
  • Alt air OFF: takeoff (always), ground in dust
  • Alt air is unfiltered — heated to melt ice
  • Suck-in door = automatic fail-safe (no pilot action)
Fuel
Two interconnected tanks per side act as one cell. Engine-driven pump primary; electric aux pump secondary (LO for vapor suppression, HI for engine-pump backup or priming, guarded). Continuous-flow injection with vapor return to the on-side tank.
  • 93 / 123 gal useable (standard / optional) · 5 gal unusable total
  • 100 / 100LL aviation grade
  • Crossfeed in level flight only · opposite selector OFF
  • 8 drains: 2 gascolator · 4 wing · 2 crossfeed (under fuselage, inboard of right flap)
Oil
Wet sump, pressure-fed. Engine-mounted filter, oil cooler with low-temperature bypass. Oil pressure also drives the propeller governor — so a single oil-system loss affects two systems.
  • 8 qt per engine · capacity range 6–8 qt operational
  • SAE 30 below 40°F OAT · SAE 50 above (multi-vis OK)
  • Winterization plate — remove above 50°F OAT
  • Min 10 PSI · max 100 PSI · max temp 240°F
Propellers & Governors
Both prop types: oil pressure drives toward HIGH RPM (fine pitch). Hartzell two-blade uses nitrogen + counterweights toward low RPM/feather. McCauley three-blade uses a compression spring + counterweights. Loss of oil pressure tends to feather the prop in this airplane.
  • Feathering takes ~6 seconds
  • Centrifugal lock prevents feather below 800 RPM
  • Synchrophaser MANUAL/OFF for taxi, takeoff, landing, single-engine
  • Avoid 2000–2200 RPM > 32" continuous · avoid 1700–2100 ground in cross/tail wind > 10 kt

Emergencies · powerplant When something goes wrong

Every engine emergency follows the same logic: aviate, identify, decide, secure. Maintain control first. Identify which engine. Decide whether to restore or secure. Then complete the securing sequence. The memory drills below give you the items and the decision points.

Engine Securing · Feathering Memory
After failure is identified, restoration attempts have failed, RPM still above 800.

Restore first

  1. Mixture — as required
  2. Fuel selector — CROSSFEED
  3. Magnetos — left or right only (mag check)
  4. Alternate air — ON
  5. Aux fuel pump — unlatch, HI · if no power restored, OFF

If no restart · feather (above 800 RPM, ≥ 76 KIAS)

  1. Mixture · Prop · Throttle — all forward (40" max)
  2. Flaps · Gear — retract
  3. Identify inop engine — yaw direction = nose toward dead
  4. Throttle inop — retard (verify loss of power)
  5. Mixture inop — idle cut-off
  6. Prop inop — FEATHER
  7. Trim — 5° bank toward operative engine
  8. Aux pumps — OFF (except eng-pump fail on operating side)
  9. Magnetos inop — OFF
  10. Cowl flap inop — close · operative as required
  11. Alternator inop — OFF
  12. Electrical load — reduce
  13. Fuel selector inop — OFF · operative on X-FEED if needed
After feather, ALT, GYRO, and OIL annunciator lights stay on for the dead engine — that's normal. The pumps and alternator on that side are no longer being driven.
Engine Failure on Takeoff Below 85 KIAS
Power loss during ground roll or just after lift-off, gear still down.
  1. Throttles — CLOSE both immediately
  2. Land if airborne
  3. Stop straight ahead
  4. If runway inadequate — max braking · master OFF · fuel selectors OFF
Below 85 KIAS the airplane cannot achieve a positive single-engine climb gradient with gear down, possible flaps, and a windmilling prop. Cleanup takes time you don't have.
Engine Failure on Takeoff 85 KIAS or above
Power loss during takeoff with 85+ KIAS achieved, gear still down.

Adequate runway: Close both throttles, land, stop straight ahead.

Inadequate runway: Pilot decision based on load, density altitude, obstructions, weather, and competence.

If continuing: Maintain heading and airspeed · feather inop engine · retract gear when climb is established.

During short-field takeoff with 25° flaps, the airplane is momentarily below Vmc. If the engine fails below Vmc it is mandatory to retard the operating throttle and lower the nose immediately to maintain control.
Engine Failure in Flight Above 66 KIAS

Concept Why single-engine is hard

The Seneca II is a docile light twin, but every twin is uncompromising about one thing: asymmetric thrust. With one engine producing full power and the other producing drag (windmilling prop, no thrust), the airplane wants to yaw toward the dead engine. Three things have to be true to maintain controlled flight:

  1. Enough airspeed for the rudder to bite (above Vmc).
  2. Enough bank toward the operating engine (5°) to use horizontal lift to offset the yaw.
  3. The dead-engine prop feathered — windmilling drag is the largest drag source on the airplane.

Lose any one of those and you're working below the airplane's design margin. Lose all three and you're in a Vmc roll or spin — recovery may not be possible at low altitude.

The Seneca II altitude paradox. In a normally-aspirated twin, Vmc decreases with altitude because engine power falls off. In the turbocharged Seneca II, the engine maintains full rated power up to ~12,000 ft — so the asymmetric thrust doesn't go away with altitude, and Vmc can be higher up high than down low. The most dangerous altitude is where Vmc and stall speed converge — directional control loss happens at the same instant as the stall, and a spin can result. This is why §4.49 has you reduce power on the demo engine rather than use full takeoff power, and why the demo is done above 3500 ft AGL.

For the same reason, the abbreviated emergency checklist on this airplane keeps you above 76 KIAS (Vsse) for any deliberate engine cut, and 85 KIAS as the abort floor on takeoff. These aren't arbitrary numbers — they're the buffer between you and the spin.

Module 02 · Landing Gear & Hydraulics

Gear & Hydraulics

Hydraulic power pack · electric reversible pump · squat switch · indicator system · emergency free-fall extension. No mechanical backup — gravity is your backup.

The Seneca II uses a self-contained hydraulic power pack in the nose: pump, motor, reservoir, valves, all in one assembly. The pump is reversible — it can pressurize either the up-side or the down-side of the gear cylinders depending on selector position, eliminating the need for two pumps. Module 03 · Electrical System

Electrical

Twin 65-amp alternators · single 35 Ah battery · 14-volt bus · solid-state regulators · overvoltage protection · external power option. Two paths to power, one path to ground.

How the boots work · pressure or suction?

The pneumatic boots run on the suction side of the same engine-driven pumps that pressurize the gyro instruments. In their default state, suction holds the boots flat against the leading edges. When you press the SURFACE DE-ICE momentary switch, a 6-second cycle pressurizes the boots, breaking ice off the leading edge. After 6 seconds, suction returns and the boots streamline. The same pumps serve gyros (pressure) and boots (suction) — they are not redundant systems.

How the prop deice cycles

Electrothermal pads bonded to the blade leading edges, energized through brushes and slip rings.

  • Hartzell two-blade: heats outboard halves first, then inboard halves, alternating R then L. Sequence: R-out → R-in → L-out → L-in.
  • McCauley three-blade: heats entire R prop pads (90 sec), then entire L (90 sec).

Heat reduces blade-ice adhesion · centrifugal force throws ice clear. The prop deicer ammeter needle should read in the shaded portion of the gauge during operation.

All-moving stabilator · Frise ailerons · mechanical flaps · cable-actuated. Conventional, predictable, no autopilot in the base POH.

The empennage uses an all-moving stabilator (slab type) with an anti-servo / trim tab on the trailing edge — the tab moves in the same direction as the stabilator, larger travel, to add longitudinal stability and prevent over-sensitivity. Ailerons are Frise type — leading edge extends into the airstream when deflected upward, adding drag and reducing adverse yaw.

Procedures

General Emergencies

Emergencies that don't belong to one specific system: emergency descent, spin recovery, door open in flight, engine failure with cabin/cargo doors removed.

Emergency Descent Memory
Rapid descent required — fire, decompression, oxygen failure at altitude.
  1. Throttles — closed
  2. Propellers — full FORWARD
  3. Mixture — as required for smooth operation
  4. Landing gear — extend
  5. Airspeed — 129 KIAS
Gear extension at 129 (Vle/Vlo) adds drag without overstressing. Props full forward provides additional drag. Time of useful consciousness at 25,000 ft is ~3 minutes — descend to ≤ 12,500 ft on oxygen-system failure.
Spin Recovery Memory · spins prohibited
Inadvertent spin entry · airplane is not certified for intentional spins.
  1. Throttles — idle
  2. Rudder — full opposite spin direction
  3. Control wheel — release back pressure · if nose doesn't drop, push full forward
  4. Ailerons — neutral
  5. Rudder — neutral when rotation stops
  6. Recover from dive with smooth back pressure · do not exceed 3.8 g
Spins are placarded prohibited and demonstrated SE stall recovery requires 500 ft loss and 15° pitch — assume spin entry will lose more.
Door Open on Takeoff or in Flight Memory
Door pops open in flight — usually noisy and surprising, rarely actually dangerous.
  1. Fly the airplane normally · this is not an immediate emergency
  2. Best plan: return to land and close on the ground
  3. If desired to close inflight: airspeed 85–94 KIAS · open storm window · pull door closed · latch upper
  4. If unclosable: airspeed 85–107 KIAS to avoid buffet · flight can continue safely
An open door is loud but the airplane flies normally. Don't get distracted from flying. Most pilots fixate and forget gear, flaps, or speed — this is the trap, not the door itself.
Engine Failure · Doors Removed Vmca = 67 KIAS
Engine failure during operations with rear cabin or cargo doors removed (skydiving, photo, etc.).
  1. Vmca becomes 67 KIAS in this configuration
  2. Below 67 — reduce power on operating engine, apply rudder for control
  3. Otherwise treat as normal engine failure
Removed doors disturb airflow over the empennage, slightly degrading rudder authority. The 1-knot Vmca penalty reflects that.

Flow 3 Engine failure on takeoff · the abort decision

This is the most time-critical engine emergency in multi-engine flying. The decision is binary and pivots on one number: 85 KIAS abort floor (Seneca II POH §3.7). Below it, abort is mandatory. Above it, the pilot has a choice based on runway remaining.

Frame F01 · DECIDE

Engine fails on the takeoff roll

Asymmetric thrust appears instantly. Recognize, then look at the airspeed indicator. The number determines what happens next.

Below 85 → ABORT (mandatory) Above 85 + runway → ABORT (judgment) Above 85 + no runway → CONTINUE
Why the floor exists Below 85 KIAS, the airplane cannot achieve a positive single-engine climb after retracting gear and flaps. Cleanup time consumes altitude that doesn't exist. Aborting on remaining runway is the only safe option.

Flow 4 Engine failure in flight · cruise to single-engine cruise

Cruise altitude. One engine fails — the airplane yaws. The drill (Seneca II POH §3.7): aviate · identify · verify · decide (restart vs commit feather) · secure · trim · communicate · plan. Restart only above Vsse 76 KIAS. Restoration items test each likely cause in sequence.

Frame I01 · Recognize

Engine fails · aviate first

Yaw, asymmetric thrust noise, possible power loss indication. Rudder against the yaw — typically a lot. Pitch for Vyse 89. Bank 5° toward the operating engine.

Rudder · pitch · 5° bank Vyse 89 Stay above Vsse 76
Don't yet Don't touch any throttle yet. Don't reach for any switch. Fly the airplane first. Identify and verify come next, in that order.
Frame I02 · Identify

Identify · dead foot, dead engine

The rudder you're not pressing is the dead side. Say it out loud: "Left foot dead, left engine dead." Don't trust gauges first — they can mislead. Trust the rudder feedback.

Out loud — dead foot, dead engine
Why out loud Verbalizing forces conscious recognition. Reduces the chance of mis-identifying the engine. The next step (verify) confirms — but identify must come first.
Frame I03 · Verify

Verify · retard suspected throttle

Slowly retard the throttle of the engine you identified as dead. If yaw and noise are unchanged → confirmed dead engine. If yaw worsens or noise drops → wrong engine. Restore that throttle and identify the other side.

No change → CORRECT engine More yaw → WRONG engine
Why slow Sudden retardation of an operating engine compounds asymmetric thrust and can drive the airplane below Vmca instantly. Slow retardation gives time to feel the difference.
Frame I04 · Decide

Decide · restart attempt or commit feather

If above Vsse 76 KIAS and time permits, work the restoration drill (POH §3.7). Each item tests one possible cause. If none of them restore power, the engine isn't coming back through pilot action — feather before RPM falls below 800.

Mixture · Fuel sel CROSSFEED · Mags L/R · Alt air · Aux pump 800 RPM = feather lockout
Pattern of test Each item tests a possible failure mode. Mixture → fuel/air ratio. Crossfeed → on-side fuel supply. Mags → ignition. Alt air → induction blockage. Aux pump → engine-driven pump. If none help, the cause is mechanical and unrecoverable in flight.
Below Vsse Skip restart attempts. Commit to feather immediately. The risk of Vmc loss outweighs the chance of restart at low speed.
Frame I05 · Feather

Feather & secure

Mixture idle cut-off. Prop control to FEATHER (back of quadrant). Throttle close. Wait for blade rotation to slow and stop. Then secure: mags off, alternator off, fuel selector off, electrical loads reduced.

Mixture cut-off Prop FEATHER Throttle close Mags · Alt · Fuel · OFF
Feather time ~6 seconds for prop to stop. Watch for rotation to cease. If prop continues windmilling, feather hasn't engaged — possibly below 800 RPM lockout. Prepare for sustained drag.
Frame I06 · Trim & plan

Trim, communicate, plan

Trim out rudder force (can be 30+ lb without trim). Maintain 5° bank toward operating engine. Declare emergency. Pick a destination — nearest suitable airport with adequate runway and weather.

Rudder trim full toward live 5° bank — ball slightly off-center toward live MAYDAY · declare
Single-engine cruise Climb performance ~100 fpm at sea level — much less at altitude. Plan descent. Monitor remaining engine carefully (CHTs, oil temp/pressure, fuel flow) — it's working harder. Cross-feed if fuel imbalance develops.
Drift-down altitude Single-engine service ceiling is ~3650 ft (Seneca II POH §4.43). Above that, aircraft will descend until reaching density-altitude where 100 fpm climb is available. Plan terrain accordingly.

Flow 5 Engine fire in flight · the 7-step procedure

Engine fire is rare but immediate. Per Seneca II POH §3.9, the procedure removes each leg of the fire triangle in sequence: fuel · air · heat. Heater and defroster come off because they share the fuel system on the affected side.

Frame Fr01 · Recognize

Engine fire indication

Visual flame, smoke from cowl, fire warning, unusual smell, abnormal engine indications (oil pressure drop, EGT/CHT spike). Don't waste time confirming what's already obvious — go directly to the drill.

Don't troubleshoot — drill
The fire triangle Fuel + air + heat = fire. The procedure systematically removes each: fuel selector cuts fuel, throttle restricts air, feather stops combustion-driving rotation. Heater off because the heater is itself a combustion device on the same fuel.
Frame Fr02 · Cut fuel/air

Steps 1 & 2 · Fuel OFF, throttle close

Identify the burning engine first. Then: fuel selector OFF on the affected engine — cuts fuel feeding the fire. Throttle close on the affected engine — restricts airflow into the engine.

Fuel selector — affected side OFF Throttle — affected side CLOSE
Identify before acting Cutting fuel to the wrong engine puts you single-engine on the operating side, with a fire still burning on the other. Same identify-verify discipline as engine failure: dead foot if running rough, or visual cue (which side is smoking).
Frame Fr03 · Feather/mixture

Steps 3 & 4 · Prop FEATHER, mixture idle cut-off

Prop control to FEATHER — stops blade rotation. Eliminates pumping fresh air through the cylinders and stops compression-induced heat. Mixture idle cut-off — eliminates any residual fuel in the manifold.

Prop — FEATHER Mixture — IDLE CUT-OFF
Heat removal A windmilling prop continues to drive the engine, which drives heat. Stopping rotation is essential. Watch for blades to stop within ~6 seconds.
Frame Fr04 · Heater off

Steps 5 & 6 · Heater OFF, defroster OFF

The Janitrol-style cabin heater is itself a combustion device, fed from the same fuel system. Even a successfully secured engine can leave a fuel-fed heater fire. Both heater and defroster controls go OFF.

Heater — OFF Defroster — OFF
Why both The defroster shares the heater plumbing on the Seneca. Leaving defroster on can supply combustion air to the heater even with heater control off. Both must be off to fully isolate.
Frame Fr05 · Land

Step 7 · Land

If fire continues despite the secured engine — terrain permitting, land. Off-airport is acceptable; structural integrity may be compromised. If the fire is out, continue to nearest suitable airport. Declare and request priority.

MAYDAY · Engine fire Nearest suitable airport Off-airport if structural concern
Sideslip away from fire Some POHs/training advocate sideslipping to keep flames away from the fuselage. POH §3.9 wording governs. If structural integrity is in doubt, expedite the descent and accept off-airport landing — better controlled forced landing than waiting for wing failure.
Fire out, can continue Single-engine cruise procedures apply (Flow 4, frame I06). Plan landing at suitable airport. Brief gear, flaps, single-engine speeds. Single-engine final 91 KIAS / 25° flaps.

Propellers
When can you NOT feather
Below 800 RPM
Centrifugal lock pin engages — by design, so the prop doesn't feather every shutdown. In flight: commit to feather BEFORE RPM drops below 800.
Normal feather check during run-up
≤ 300 RPM drop
At 1000 RPM, cycle prop control toward feather and back. The lock pin keeps you from actually feathering — you're verifying pitch travel.
What causes prop to high RPM · low RPM
Both prop types: oil pressure drives toward HIGH RPM (fine pitch). Hartzell uses nitrogen + counterweights toward LOW RPM/feather. McCauley uses spring + counterweights.
Loss of oil pressure → tends to feather in this airplane (opposite of most singles). Governor decides, oil executes, opposing force is type-specific.
Landing gear
Hydraulic pressure source
Electric reversible hydraulic pump (power pack), in the nose, accessible through forward baggage compartment
No mechanical hand-pump backup. Backup extension is by gravity (free-fall).
Why not reverse selector in transit
May damage the electric pump motor
Wait for full travel before reversing direction.
How is inadvertent ground retraction prevented
Squat (safety) switch on left main oleo strut · opens pump electrical circuit on the ground
Belt and suspenders: throttle micro-switch also triggers the gear horn if selector is UP on the ground.
Manual gear extension
CBs · master ON · alts check · nav lights OFF · clip down · ≤ 85 KIAS · selector to GEAR DOWN LOCKED · pull emergency knob · 3 greens · leave knob out until on jacks
Pulling the knob releases the hydraulic pressure holding the gear up. Gravity, airflow, and downlock springs do the rest.
Gear light won't illuminate on approach
Nav lights OFF (auto-dim trap) · swap bulb · recycle gear · manual extension if needed
Three causes: not actually locked, bulb burned out, or indicator-system fault. Bulbs are interchangeable for swap testing.
When does gear horn sound
MP < ~14" Hg on either engine with gear not locked · OR selector UP on the ground
90 cycles per minute beep · different from continuous-tone stall warning.

Go-around procedure
Full takeoff power both engines (40 max) · positive climb · flaps retract · gear UP · cowl flaps adjust

Emergencies
When use alternate air
Suspected induction icing · filter blockage · engine roughness in IMC/visible moisture · engine failure in icing · run-up cycle check
Don't use on takeoff (always primary filtered) · don't use on ground in dust (alt air is unfiltered).
Restart procedure prior to feathering
Mixture · selector X-FEED · mags L or R · alt air ON · aux pump HI then OFF if no help · maintain ≥ 76 KIAS
Each step tests one possible cause: F/A ratio, fuel supply, ignition, induction, engine-pump. None work → secure and feather.
Why abort below Vyse even if airborne
Below 85 KIAS the airplane cannot achieve a positive single-engine climb gradient with gear down, possible flaps, and a windmilling prop
By the time you've cleaned up the airplane and feathered, you'd be below Vyse without the altitude to recover. POH mandates throttles closed below 85; above 85 it's pilot decision.
Engine fire in flight
Fuel selector OFF · throttle close · prop FEATHER · mixture idle cut-off · heater OFF · defroster OFF · land if terrain permits if fire continues
Each step removes one element. Heater is a combustion heater on the same fuel.
Electrical failure in flight
Single alt: cycle switch · reset CB once · reduce loads · continue. Both: cycle switches one at a time, leave on the better one. Both inop: battery only · ~115 min day VFR / ~35 min night IFR · land ASAP.
In icing: drop the heated windshield first (largest single load). Compass error may exceed 10° on battery alone.
Propeller overspeed
Throttle reduce · prop full aft · airspeed reduce · feather if RPM control lost
⚠ schoolNot a discrete §3 procedure in this POH. School-taught. Both prop types resist mechanical overspeed (Hartzell nitrogen / McCauley spring).
Vmc
Minimum altitude for Vmc demo
3500 ft AGL
Per §4.49. Demo approaches uncontrolled flight; this altitude gives spin/stall recovery margin.
Vmc recovery procedure
Reduce power on the operating engine · lower the nose · accelerate to Vsse 76 KIAS
Asymmetric thrust IS the cause — reduce thrust to restore controllability. Lowering the nose recovers airspeed. Re-apply power above Vsse.
Factors affecting Vmc
Power · altitude · bank angle · weight · CG · configuration · prop state (windmill vs feather) · OAT/density · operating-engine power available
Seneca quirk: turbocharged engine maintains rated power up to 12,000 ft, so Vmc doesn't drop with altitude like normally-aspirated twins. The most dangerous altitude is where Vmc and stall speed converge — Vmc demo done with reduced power above 3500 AGL for that reason.
